### Logistics model
- **Asset-light at long-haul.** DTNA does not run an enterprise private fleet for inbound components or finished-vehicle delivery; specialized dealer-delivery convoys and 3PLs move finished trucks
- **Saddle-back outbound** for finished trucks (engine + cab + chassis = a truck that drives itself off the lot) — the yard problem is less "trailers waiting" and more "completed trucks marshalling for dealer dispatch"
- **Inbound JIT** for components — Saltillo runs 500+ trailers in the yard at peak per the PINC case study, almost entirely inbound material
- **Rail-out option for finished vehicles** from select plants — Cleveland NC sits on rail, Saltillo runs into US via heavy-haul truck convoys
- **Aftermarket flows are separate** — PDC outbound is dealer parts orders, more cadenced and CPG-shaped than the lumpy finished-vehicle flow

Capabilities deployed: passive UHF RFID on trailers, mobile readers, yard move management, dock-door utilization, dwell/aging visibility, detention reporting. Reported outcomes: **50% reduction in carrier detention fees**, **50% reduction in yard drivers**, **3 trailers/hour → improved** yard-tractor productivity baseline. This is **confirmed-customer**, not inferred

## 3. Current State, Initiatives, and Pain Points

### Q1 2026 was bad (tariffs + demand softness, both in one quarter)
- **Group EBIT halved** to €498M (Q1 2026) from €1.08B (Q1 2025)
- **DTNA revenue −29%** to $4.51B (from $6.35B); **DTNA EBIT −73%** to $245.6M (from $914.2M)
- **DTNA Q1 sales: 29,432 trucks and buses, −24.5% YoY** — lowest Q1 since 2010
- **Tariff headwind in the "low triple-digit million" euros** in Q1 2026, first full quarter of tariff impact reflected
- Management is forecasting on "near-term, temporary disruption" of supply chains (Iran-conflict reference in disclosure) and continued USMCA operation as base case

### …but the bottom is in — order intake is +85% YoY in Q1 2026
- **Q1 2026 incoming orders +85% YoY** at Trucks North America
- **Q2 2026 DTNA sales pacing 50% ahead of Q1**
- **Translation:** the production network just spent a quarter undershooting capacity; now demand is returning hard. The squeeze on the manufacturing footprint is not "we need more capacity" — it is "we need to ramp the existing 9 plants without burning the goodwill we still have left with carriers and dealers after a soft year"

This is the highest-leverage moment in five years for a yard pitch to Allen. The plants are about to be asked to do more, with the same dock doors and the same yard footprint, on a fleet (PINC) that has been in the network for **14 years** and was specified into a problem that looked different in 2012.

### Tariff-amplified per-minute math
Same math as Caterpillar: when net tariff impact is a low-triple-digit million euros per quarter on DTNA alone, **every minute of dwell amplifies the per-unit landed cost** of components moving from Mexico (Saltillo) to the US plants and of US-made trucks moving into the dealer network. Yard dwell becomes a margin lever, not just an ops lever.

### The 850,000th-truck moment (July 2025, Cleveland NC)
Cleveland NC delivered its 850,000th truck in July 2025 — a milestone press release moment that doubles as a signal that **the Cleveland yard has 70 years of layered process under it**. Heritage plants with that much accumulated workflow are exactly where a 2012-era RFID-on-trailers deployment ages worst — the gate, dock, marshalling, and convoy-dispatch flows have evolved around the YMS, not the YMS around them.

### eCascadia / eM2 series production at Portland — a new yard problem
Series production of the **Freightliner eM2 battery-electric** started at Portland (joining the eCascadia). Battery-electric truck assembly introduces battery handling, charging-bay marshalling, and HV-component inbound logistics — **new yard surfaces that the 2012-era PINC config was not designed to track**. Greenfield network-tier rebuild work is easier here than retrofitting Cleveland.

### Thomas Built High Point capacity expansion (December 2025)
The Type A Minotour expansion at the High Point Thomas Built plant adds a new dimension to the manufacturing network — school bus volume growth alongside Freightliner core. More volume, same yard.

### Whitestown RDC (August 2023) — adjacent, not Allen's but worth knowing
605k sq ft, opened to replenish 10 PDCs. This is the strongest signal in the public record that DTNA's logistics function has been **reorganized for network throughput in the last three years**. The same network logic applied to plant yards — moving from 9 site-level YMS deployments to a network operating model — is the modernization wedge.

### Sustainability angle `[unverified specifics]`
Daimler Truck has decarbonization commitments and is the company that just put the Freightliner eM2 into series production. Logistics-side decarbonization (idle reduction, yard EV transition) is a credible secondary line of argument but not the main one — the main one is throughput-during-recovery.

## 4. YardFlow Opportunity — Daimler Truck NA

### The pitch shape: MODERNIZATION (Kaleris built site-level YMS for 2012's problem)
This is the same pitch shape as Caterpillar in spirit but **stronger on documented evidence**. Cat's YMS status is `[unverified]` in public sources; DTNA's PINC deployment is *named, documented, and 14 years old.* The Kaleris case study itself is the proof — they are **educated buyers** who already understand the category. The opportunity is not to educate them; it is to **reframe the unit of analysis from site to network**.

The argument in one sentence:
> Kaleris built site-level yard control 10–15 years ago when standardization was the unsolved problem. The opportunity now is moving from site-level visibility to a network-level operating model — coordinating gate, dock, sequenced inbound, and finished-vehicle outbound across Portland, Cleveland, Mt. Holly, Gastonia, Gaffney, High Point, Saltillo, and the Detroit Diesel engine plant as one orchestrated system, not nine isolated yard logs.

### Why "site → network" is the real wedge
The PINC deployment was a brilliant 2012 solution to a 2012 problem: drivers walking the yard with clipboards reading trailer serial numbers. RFID solved that. **What it did not solve, and what nothing in the 2012 architecture was designed to solve:**

- Cross-site sequencing of inbound components (engine ship from Detroit → Cleveland line readiness window)
- Saltillo-to-US tariff-amplified inbound coordination (when every truck across the border carries an extra fee, the lane has to be choreographed not just visible)
- Battery-electric mfg yard surfaces at Portland that didn't exist in 2012
- Finished-vehicle marshalling and dealer-convoy dispatch as a network problem, not a per-plant problem
- Exception management *between* plants — when Cleveland is dock-blocked, where does the inbound Detroit engine load divert?

YardFlow's value vs. PINC is not "we tag trailers better." It is "we coordinate the nine yards as a network and feed that coordination signal into the planning layer."

### The math (plant tier)
- **9 manufacturing plants × ~50 trailer movements/day average** `[unverified — modeled estimate; Saltillo alone runs 500+ trailers, so the network-wide daily movement count is plausibly higher]` ≈ **450 trailer movements/day** at the conservative end
- Cut 20 minutes off network-coordinated turn time → **150 hours/day saved** → **~55,000 hours/year of driver + equipment time** released
- Per-unit value at heavy-truck OEM economics is well above CPG benchmarks — heavy-haul, oversized-component, and finished-truck per-trailer value runs 5–20× a CPG dry-goods trailer
- Tariff multiplier on top: in a quarter where the company just lost €500M+ of EBIT to tariffs, the value of any minute saved that compounds across the Saltillo border lane is asymmetric

### Five reasons Jeff Allen could care
1. **The PINC deployment is 14 years old.** Even Allen's own org chart didn't exist in its current shape when Saltillo went live. A network-tier reset is overdue
2. **Order intake +85% YoY means production ramp is the binding constraint in Q2-Q4 2026.** Lifting effective throughput per dock door at zero capex is the cleanest answer to "ramp the 9 plants"
3. **The Detroit Diesel-to-Cascadia coordination problem is his native problem.** Allen ran Detroit for 25 years before moving to DTNA. The cross-site inbound-engine choreography is the kind of problem he viscerally understands and will fund a fix for
4. **eCascadia / eM2 series production is a greenfield yard surface at Portland.** Specify network orchestration into the EV-assembly yard layout while concrete is still wet — Allen owns Portland
5. **PINC/Kaleris is a 14-year incumbent. They are educated buyers.** The sale is "let's evolve" not "let's start" — meaningfully shorter cycle than a cold-start deal at the same size

### Where this is *not* a fit (be honest)
- **Allen will not displace PINC at Saltillo on a vendor pitch.** Saltillo is the original deployment site, 14-year emotional investment, and the most-cited internal proof point. **Lead with the network layer above PINC, not with PINC replacement.** Position YardFlow as orchestration on top, with PINC site signals as one input. Replacement, if it happens, is the second or third year
- **The parts depot network is Backeberg's, not Allen's.** A pitch to Allen built around the 10 PDCs and the Whitestown RDC will land in the wrong inbox. If the warm path opens to Backeberg, that is a parallel motion with parts-network framing
- **DTNA is in a margin trough.** Capex-heavy proposals will not get oxygen in 2026. **Lead with no-capex, network-tier-only deployment** — software layer on top of existing PINC infrastructure
- **Long courtship.** Allen's profile is a structured-buyer profile. First touch May 2026; realistic first paid network-tier pilot Q1-Q2 2027 at one or two adjacent plants (Cleveland + Mt. Holly is the obvious pairing)
