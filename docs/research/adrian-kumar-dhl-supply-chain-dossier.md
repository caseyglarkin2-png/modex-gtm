## 4. The 3PL Adjustment — Why DHL Supply Chain Is a Different Kind of Target

### YardFlow's 3PL Thesis (vs. End-Shipper Pitch)
For an end-shipper like General Mills or Diageo, YardFlow is a tool they buy and operate at their own facilities. For a 3PL of DHL's scale, YardFlow is something fundamentally different:

> **A capability they design once into the DHL operating standard and resell as a managed-service component to thousands of shipper customers.**

DHL's business model already does this with every other technology layer in the building — WMS, TMS, AMRs (Locus, Boston Dynamics), vision picking, slotting, returns processing. They productize third-party tech, wrap it in operational service and engineering rigor, and bill it through to the shipper. **YardFlow fits this exact distribution model** — but for the layer of the operation that today is mostly still PINC-of-record + clipboard-execution.

### Why This Is a Partnership Conversation, Not a Vendor Sale
A standard YMS pitch says: *"Replace your spreadsheet with our software."* That's a procurement conversation.

The DHL pitch needs to say: *"Embed YardFlow into the DHL Supply Chain operating standard as the yard-execution layer — the same way you've embedded Locus and Boston Dynamics. We bring a software stack and a pricing model designed for multi-tenant 3PL deployment. You bring 1,000+ facilities, 52,000 NA associates, and customer relationships that span every Fortune-500 vertical. We split the value at the customer perimeter."*

This reframes Adrian's evaluation:
- Not: *"Do I want to buy this for my own ops?"* (procurement instinct: defer to operations)
- Instead: *"Is this a layer I can put into our solutions-design standard and bring to every new customer engagement?"* (design-engineering instinct: lean in)

### Adrian Specifically Is The Right Door
DHL's CEO (Mark Kunar) is a finance-and-strategy operator; he won't take a vendor meeting cold and he'll route this to Solutions Design anyway. DHL's COO-equivalent in NA Operations would route this to procurement. **Adrian's published methodology is: identify a customer-side need → pilot a partner technology → roll out to a customer site → productize across the customer base.** That's exactly Locus. That's exactly Boston Dynamics. That's exactly the path YardFlow needs.

His public 5-steps-to-innovation framework, the Ohio University teaching gig, the recurring conference-speaker presence — these add up to a person who **runs a structured evaluation, not a hunch-based one.** The pitch needs to read like an engineering memo, not a marketing pitch.

### Cross-Reference: The Marsha McIntosh-Hamilton (Diageo) Warm-Intro Vector
DHL operates Diageo's Plainfield, IL campus — the most-watched yard in spirits and the showcase site for DHL's own decarbonization narrative (EV yard trucks since 2015, Nikola hydrogen Class 8 since 2024). YardFlow's pitch to Marsha at Diageo includes "complete the Plainfield sustainability showcase by adding the yard-execution layer." **That same Plainfield site is operated by DHL.** Either side of the Diageo-DHL relationship can warm-intro the other into a joint conversation: "we're talking with your operator about a layer that makes the existing decarbonization investment more legible, and we'd like to scope it together."
