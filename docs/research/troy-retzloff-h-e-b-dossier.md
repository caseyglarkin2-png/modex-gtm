### Logistics Model — Why H-E-B Looks Different From the Rest of the Wave 1 List
Three structural differences worth naming:

1. **Self-operated fleet, not 3PL.** H-E-B controls the trucks, the drivers, the routes, and the dock windows. Yard delay is **internal cost, not externalized carrier cost.** Closer to Frito-Lay's DSD model than to General Mills' 3PL-dependent model.
2. **Texas-only retail concentration with a Mexico spur.** The network is geographically dense — every store is within a relatively short radius of an RSC, which compresses fleet turns and amplifies the cost of any individual yard delay
3. **Manufacturing colocated with distribution at flagship sites.** The East Side San Antonio campus is the textbook example: own-manufactured product flows from H-E-B Manufacturing across the same yard envelope as third-party inbound and store-bound outbound. The yard is shared between three traffic patterns, not one.

- **Inbound from H-E-B Manufacturing and external CPG vendors**

## 4. Known Logistics Pain Points

### 1. Mixed Yard Traffic at Flagship Campuses
The East Side San Antonio campus runs **own-manufactured inbound + third-party CPG inbound + store-bound outbound** through the same yard envelope. 200 dock doors, 180 outbound trailers/day at design — and inbound on top of that. Three traffic patterns sharing one yard is the textbook scenario where gate-in/dock-in standardization produces visible throughput gains.

### 2. Texas Geographic Concentration as a Pressure Multiplier
Every H-E-B yard delay is **immediately visible to a Texas store**, because the network is short-radius. There is no "we have other DCs that can cover" buffer the way there is at a national CPG. Yard ops at H-E-B is a same-day shelf-availability lever, not a quarterly cost-of-goods lever.

### 3. eCommerce Yard Volume Doesn't Look Like RSC Yard Volume
RSCs are built around outbound store-truck cadence to ~440 stores. eCommerce fulfillment centers are inbound-heavy from third-party CPGs and outbound-heavy in last-mile vans, micro-fulfillment refresh trucks, and curbside-pickup transfers. **Yard tooling tuned for one is not automatically tuned for the other.** As eComm capacity expands across Houston, Plano, Frisco, and Katy, H-E-B's distribution org is increasingly running two yard-pattern types in parallel.

### 4. Hurricane / Disaster-Response Tail Risk
Harvey is the marquee example; the next storm is the constant background concern. **Disaster operations are a yard problem before they are a route problem** — surge inventory has to land at a DC, get visible, and turn around fast. Any yard layer that improves day-one gate-to-dock visibility is also a disaster-response upgrade. This is unusually load-bearing at H-E-B specifically.

### 5. Multi-Temperature Trailer Legacy
Troy's own public writing is on multi-temperature trailers — frozen + produce in the same trailer envelope. Multi-temp trailers are a yard problem in their own right: a multi-temp trailer at a single-temp dock door wastes the trailer, and a single-temp trailer at a multi-temp door wastes the door. **Door-to-trailer-type matching is exactly the kind of orchestration a modern yard system handles and a paper appointment book does not.**

### 6. Vertical Integration = More Inbound Variety
H-E-B Manufacturing produces bakery, dairy, water (San Antonio Aquafina-class lines), tortillas, deli, bread, and more. Those production lines feed RSCs alongside outside CPG inbound. **Manufacturing-to-DC trailer flow is yard work that doesn't exist at all at a non-vertical CPG retailer.** It also means the gate sees a non-trivial percentage of H-E-B-owned tractors carrying H-E-B-owned trailers carrying H-E-B-owned freight — a closed-loop that should, in principle, be the easiest yard to instrument and the hardest to externalize blame for.

### 7. The Hempstead Campus Decision Window
Greenfield distribution campuses get their operational standard set in the first 18 months. Hempstead is in that window **right now**. Any yard tooling that lands on the 200th dock door at an existing brownfield site has to displace habit; any yard tooling that lands on day one at Hempstead writes the habit. The institutional buying logic at H-E-B almost certainly knows this; the question is whether the procurement timeline is open or already settled.

## 5. YardFlow Opportunity

### The Anchor Comparable
YardFlow's anchor customer is a **237-facility North American CPG network** — a Tier-1 operator that displaced an incumbent yard system to standardize gate-in → dock-in across plants and depots under contract. The closest **public** comparable is **Primo Brands**, the largest bottled-water producer in North America, where similar yard-operation rebuilds have produced documented dwell and throughput improvements.

For H-E-B specifically, the structural read:
- **440 stores fed by a tight network of ~6–10 backbone RSCs + multiple eCommerce fulfillment sites + Hempstead in build** ≈ a denser-but-smaller version of the 237-facility shape. Texas-only concentration means fewer sites, but each site carries proportionally more volume
- **Self-operated fleet** ≈ shared yard-as-operating-cost pattern with the anchor, where dwell is internal cost rather than externalized carrier cost
- **Manufacturing colocated with distribution** ≈ same yard-traffic-mixing problem as the anchor's bottling-plant + depot adjacencies
- **Active automation partnership with Swisslog inside the warehouse wall** ≈ same warehouse-automated-but-yard-manual mismatch the anchor displaced

The mismatch worth flagging: Primo Brands is a **single-category** (water) network; H-E-B is **omni-category** (ambient + frozen + produce + dairy + bakery + own-manufactured + eCommerce). The yard problem at H-E-B is meaningfully more heterogeneous. That increases the YardFlow ROI ceiling — heterogeneity is where door-to-trailer-type orchestration earns its keep — but raises the implementation effort floor.

### What YardFlow Specifically Solves at H-E-B
1. **Write the yard standard at Hempstead.** The single most leveraged conversation in the H-E-B distribution org in 2026 is what gate-to-dock looks like at Hempstead Phase 1. YardFlow's wedge is to land that conversation now and let Hempstead become the internal proof that scales to San Antonio, Houston, Temple, and Weslaco.
2. **Match doors to multi-temp trailers automatically.** The trailer-program Troy personally championed in the 1990s is a textbook orchestration problem in 2026. Door availability + trailer temp profile + dock equipment compatibility is exactly what a modern yard system resolves in software.
3. **Make the East Side San Antonio campus yard legible.** Three traffic patterns, 200 doors, 180 outbound trailers/day, plus inbound — all sharing one yard. Gate-in → dock-in standardization on a campus this size is where comparable-network lift figures translate most directly.
4. **Carry the eCommerce yard as a first-class case.** Swisslog/AutoStore inside the wall is solved. The yard outside the wall — third-party CPG inbound, last-mile outbound, store-transfer micro-fulfillment refresh — is unsolved at every public eCommerce site. YardFlow can be the standard that follows the AutoStore footprint across Houston #3, Plano, Frisco, and the next sites in queue.
5. **Be ready when the next storm lands.** Day-one yard visibility is a disaster-response upgrade. H-E-B is the company that wrote the public playbook on this. The pitch sentence almost writes itself: *"What you did at Beaumont in 2017 was a yard execution story; here is how the next one is faster."*

## 6. Outreach Strategy — Sub-C-Suite Reality

Troy is senior director. Buying authority for a network-scale yard-software contract sits at least one and probably two layers above him. There are two viable paths.

### Path A (Recommended): Bottom-Up Champion Build
Troy is the right *operator* to evaluate yard tooling and own the operational case. The play is:

1. Open a 20-minute conversation framed as **operator-to-operator on the multi-temp trailer + East Side San Antonio yard**, not as a vendor pitch
2. Earn his interest at the operational level — he has a 31-year résumé of valuing fleet and trailer arithmetic; mirror that register
3. Position YardFlow as **"the system you would have built at the Houston RSC if the tooling existed in 2005"** — flatter the operator without flattering the executive
4. Convert his interest into an **internal warm intro to the VP / SVP Supply Chain** — at H-E-B, that role has historically sat under the CEO directly given the family-run governance pattern
5. Pilot site: **Hempstead Phase 1** if accessible, **East Side San Antonio campus** as backup

If a credible introduction to H-E-B's senior supply chain executive (the chair above Troy) is available through a mutual connection, the network-scale conversation belongs there from the start. **Default to Path A.** Troy's operator profile is uncommonly favorable for a champion role — long tenure, public Harvey artifact, public multi-temp trailer artifact, RILA committee service, awards profile that emphasizes leader development. He is exactly the kind of senior director who can sponsor a pilot at one RSC and carry the case upstairs.
