### The Two-Halves Yard Pattern (Why K-C Is Different from a Food CPG)
K-C's yard ergonomics are not General Mills' ergonomics. They are not Hormel's or Smucker's either. The structural feature that matters:

| Side | Inbound | Outbound |
|---|---|---|
| **Tissue / Paper** | Massive inbound pulp and recycled-fiber rail/truck volume into integrated mills; converters take **giant paper rolls** by truck and rail | Outbound finished tissue is **cube-heavy, not weight-heavy** — trailers fill on volume long before weight, leading to high trailer count per shipped ton |
| **Personal Care (Huggies, Kotex, Pull-Ups)** | Inbound nonwovens, superabsorbents, plastics, adhesives — moderate volume, high SKU complexity | Outbound is **cube-extreme** — a trailer of diapers cubes out at ~25–30% of weight limit. **The yard implication:** Both halves of K-C's network are **trailer-count-heavy**, not tonnage-heavy. That means yard congestion is structurally worse for K-C than for a food-CPG peer at the same revenue scale, because the same dollar of finished goods moves in more physical trailers. **Yard turn-time savings compound harder at K-C than at a comparably-sized food shipper.** This is a meaningful framing distinction.

## 4. Known Logistics Pain Points

### 1. Cube-Out, Not Weight-Out (The Structural K-C Problem)
Diapers, tissue, and feminine care all cube out at well under the legal weight limit. **K-C moves more physical trailers per ton of finished goods than any food-CPG peer.** Trailer count is the yard's master variable — every trailer is a gate-in event, a dock-in event, a hostle move, an exit event. **Yard scale at K-C is structurally amplified versus peers.** Anyone who has worked a K-C DC knows this; outreach should reference it explicitly.

### 2. Mill-to-Converter Inbound (Tissue Side)
K-C's integrated tissue mills produce giant paper rolls that move to converter plants by truck and rail. **Inbound rail-and-truck flow into converters is a high-volume, high-variability yard problem** — closely analogous to Georgia-Pacific's mill-to-converter pattern, but inside one operating entity rather than across a value chain. Mill production runs 24/7; converter intake has finite dock capacity. **The yard between mill and converter is the relief valve, and right now it absorbs the variance manually.**

### 3. Powering Care Consolidation (Active)
Marinette closures and ongoing footprint rationalization mean **surviving facilities are absorbing more volume right now**. Same yard layout, more trailers. The yard is the bottleneck that determines whether the Powering Care productivity number lands or slips.

### 4. PINC/Kaleris Incumbent Friction
The briefing identifies K-C as a confirmed PINC/Kaleris customer. PINC's yard management product is the historical CPG default — and its execution-layer capability has not materially advanced since the 2022 Kaleris acquisition `[unverified — observation, not data]`. The pattern across PINC accounts is consistent: **gate-and-locate works, but in-yard execution and dock orchestration are the gap.** That is the displacement opening.

### 5. Retail Customer Scorecard Pressure
K-C sells to Walmart, Target, Costco, Sam's Club, Kroger, Amazon. **MABD penalties at these accounts compound quickly.** Yard delays at the outbound K-C DC translate directly into late retail deliveries → chargebacks → customer-scorecard reports landing on Tamera's desk. The retailer-side OTIF squeeze has tightened materially through 2024–2025; this is not a softening pressure point.

### 6. K-C Professional Channel Complexity
B2B away-from-home products (commercial tissue, wipers, PPE) move on **different cadence and trailer profiles** than retail. Many K-C plants run both retail-channel and Professional-channel outbound from the same yard. **Mixed-channel outbound is a documented yard-chaos trigger** — different appointment systems, different carrier mixes, different load-build rules, all converging on shared dock doors.

### 7. Carrier Relationship in a Cube-Heavy Network
K-C is **3PL/contract-carrier dependent for most over-the-road moves**. Because trailers cube out before they weight out, carriers running K-C lanes earn less per cube-mile than carriers running denser freight. **When carriers can choose their shippers (tight capacity markets), K-C yards need to be friction-free or carriers re-prioritize.** Yard dwell directly impacts K-C's ability to procure capacity at acceptable rates. This is the same lever that lands at General Mills, sharpened by K-C's structural trailer-count problem.

### 8. International Spin-off Integration
The standalone international Personal Care entity will require its own supply chain operating model through 2025–2026. **Carve-out integrations are when yard standardization decisions get re-opened across the parent.** The window to enter the NA-focused entity with a standardized gate-in → dock-in approach is now, before the parent re-baselines its operating standards post-spin.

### The Displacement Opening

1. **PINC's execution-layer roadmap has not moved at the pace that Powering Care requires.** K-C's productivity mandate is now a 2026–2027 board-visible commitment. Yard tools built for steady-state operations are not the tools you want during a footprint consolidation.
2. **The post-Kaleris-acquisition PINC product is not the focus of Kaleris's R&D dollars.** Kaleris's strategic direction has skewed toward marine/intermodal and the legacy Navis terminal-operations product line. Pure-CPG yard customers have a defensible reason to re-evaluate.

The displacement pitch at K-C is therefore **not** "you don't have a YMS." It is:

> "You bought PINC for the gate-and-locate layer in a steady-state network. The Powering Care consolidation has changed the operating problem. The execution-and-orchestration layer that consolidation requires is not the PINC product, and it is not on the Kaleris roadmap."

### The Visibility / Execution Gap (Refined for K-C)
K-C has best-in-class tools for:
- Demand planning (Blue Yonder + AI)
- Manufacturing operations (SAP S/4 + automation)
- Transportation (Blue Yonder TMS, contract carrier networks)

What K-C does **not** have (or, more precisely, does not have at the standard the Powering Care number requires):

This is the YardFlow-shaped hole at K-C. The PINC deployment doesn't fill it; it predates the problem.
