## 2. Keurig Dr Pepper Supply Chain — Operational Footprint

### Company Overview
- **Revenue:** ~$15.3B (FY2025) — 3rd largest beverage company in North America behind Coca-Cola and PepsiCo
- **Brands:** 125+ brands — Dr Pepper, 7UP, Snapple, Canada Dry, Sunkist, A&W, Mott's, Clamato, Core Water, Bai, K-Cup pods, Keurig brewers, Green Mountain Coffee
- **HQ:** Burlington, MA (Keurig) / Plano, TX (Dr Pepper legacy) — dual HQ structure
- **Employees:** ~28,000

### The Dual Distribution Model — KDP's Defining Complexity

This is the single most important thing to understand about KDP's supply chain:

#### Direct Store Delivery (DSD) Network
- **~13,000 distribution routes** across the US serving ~500,000+ retail points of sale
- DSD handles Dr Pepper, 7UP, Canada Dry, Sunkist, A&W, Squirt, Big Red (in some markets)
- Drivers deliver directly to grocery stores, convenience stores, gas stations, restaurants, vending machines
- KDP operates the **largest DSD beverage network** that is not owned by Coca-Cola or PepsiCo
- DSD relies on a vast network of **company-owned and independent bottler/distributor warehouses** — trailers are constantly moving between plants, regional DSD warehouses, and local depots
- **Yard impact:** Each DSD depot is a high-frequency, low-dwell yard operation — trailers in, cases staged, route trucks loaded, empties returned. Dock scheduling is critical because routes have fixed morning departure windows

#### Warehouse Distribution Network
- K-Cup pods, Keurig brewers, and an expanding set of beverages (Snapple, Core, Bai) go through **traditional warehouse/DC distribution** to retail via retailer DCs
- K-Cups ship primarily from manufacturing plants in **Vermont (Waterbury), South Carolina, Georgia, and Texas** to retailer distribution centers nationwide
- This is a high-volume, palletized, LTL/TL environment — very different from DSD
- **Yard impact:** K-Cup plants run 24/7 production lines and need **continuous trailer availability** at the dock. Any dock bottleneck directly throttles production output

#### The Hybrid/Transition Zone
- KDP has been **actively shifting certain brands from DSD to warehouse delivery** to reduce cost (notably some Snapple, Bai, and Core Water products)
- This DSD-to-warehouse transition creates **temporary chaos** in the logistics network as volumes shift between distribution channels
- Some facilities handle BOTH DSD and warehouse shipments — creating mixed-mode yard complexity

### Manufacturing Footprint

### Fleet & Transportation

## 4. Known Logistics Pain Points — Where YardFlow Fits

### Pain Point 1: DSD Depot Yard Chaos
- **~100+ DSD depots** each with limited dock doors (typically 4-12 doors per location)
- DSD depots have **brutal morning time windows** — all route trucks must be loaded and dispatched by 5-6 AM for first deliveries
- Inbound line-haul trailers from plants arrive overnight and must be **staged, prioritized, and unloaded in sequence** to support route truck loading
- Empty trailer management is a constant headache — empties pile up, blocking dock doors and yard space
- **YardFlow value:** Real-time visibility into trailer location/status at each depot, automated dock door assignment, priority sequencing for time-critical route-loading

### Pain Point 2: K-Cup Plant Continuous Production
- K-Cup production lines run **24/7** with extremely high throughput — a single line can produce 1,000+ pods per minute
- Finished goods flow directly from production to palletization to trailer loading
- If no empty trailer is available at the dock, **production must slow or stop** — this is the "yard is the constraint" scenario at its most extreme
- K-Cup plants in SC, VT, and VA likely have **20-40 dock doors** each with continuous trailer cycling
- **YardFlow value:** Predictive trailer availability, automated yard moves to ensure an empty trailer is always ready at the dock, elimination of production stoppages from yard bottlenecks

### Pain Point 3: Dual-Mode Facility Complexity
- Some KDP facilities handle **both DSD and warehouse outbound shipments**
- DSD trailers need to be loaded in route-stop sequence; warehouse trailers are palletized loads going to retailer DCs
- Different trailer types, different dock requirements, different priority levels — all sharing the same yard and dock doors
- **YardFlow value:** Separate workflows and priority rules for DSD vs. warehouse trailers within the same yard, ensuring neither mode blocks the other

### Pain Point 4: Seasonal Volume Swings
- **Summer:** Dr Pepper, 7UP, Sunkist, Canada Dry volume spikes 30-50% — DSD network under maximum strain
- **Fall/Holiday:** K-Cup sales surge for holiday gifting and cold-weather consumption — warehouse distribution maxed out
- **Super Bowl / NCAA Tournament:** Short, intense demand spikes for Dr Pepper (official NCAA sponsor)
- Seasonal peaks stress yard capacity at both plants and DCs — **temporary trailer overflow, yard congestion, longer turn times**
- **YardFlow value:** Dynamic yard capacity management, surge planning, temporary overflow yard coordination

### Pain Point 5: Post-Merger System Fragmentation
- Legacy Keurig and legacy Dr Pepper Snapple facilities still run **different WMS, TMS, and yard management processes** in some locations
- SAP S/4HANA migration is standardizing the ERP layer, but operational yard processes at individual facilities may still be **manual or inconsistent**
- Some facilities use paper-based yard checks, CB radio for yard jockeys, and manual dock assignment
- **YardFlow value:** Standardized YMS across all facility types (plants, DCs, DSD depots) — accelerates operational integration that the SAP migration alone won't address

### Pain Point 6: DSD-to-Warehouse Transition Disruption
- As brands shift from DSD to warehouse distribution, trailer volumes at affected facilities **change unpredictably**
- DSD depots lose volume (but still need to operate); warehouse DCs gain volume (and may not have enough dock capacity)
- The transition period creates **temporary inefficiency** that a flexible YMS can help manage
