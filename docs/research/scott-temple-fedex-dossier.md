## 2. FedEx Supply Chain — Operational Footprint

### Business Unit Overview
- **Parent:** FedEx Corporation (NYSE: FDX)
- **Segment:** Operates under **FedEx Logistics** business unit since January 2019; **distinct from FedEx Express, FedEx Ground (now consolidated as Federal Express), and FedEx Freight (spinning off June 1, 2026 as FDXF)**
- **HQ:** 100 Papercraft Park, **Pittsburgh, PA 15238** — inherited from GENCO
- **Origin:** Acquired as **GENCO** for **$1.4B in January 2015**, rebranded FedEx Supply Chain in 2017
- **Scale:** ~**11,000 employees**, ~**130 warehouse and DC operations** in North America, ~**35M sq ft** of warehouse space
- **Returns volume:** **475M returns processed annually** (2025 figure; up from 358M in earlier company materials — material growth)
- **Direct-to-consumer:** **500K+ DTC shipments per day**

### Verticals Served
1. **Technology & Electronics** — repair, refurbishment, e-waste (heritage GENCO strength)
2. **Retail & E-Commerce** — fulfillment, returns processing
3. **Consumer & Industrial Goods** — warehousing and distribution
4. **Healthcare** — fastest-growing vertical; FedEx booked **$500M+ in healthcare quality agreements in FY2024** and another **~$400M in FY2025** [FedEx-wide figures, not Supply Chain-specific, but Supply Chain is the warehousing arm capturing this growth]

### What Makes This Account Different
Most YardFlow targets are **shippers** — CPGs, retailers, manufacturers operating their own (or 3PL-managed) DCs. **FedEx Supply Chain is a 3PL operator**: their facilities are operated for *other* companies' inventory. This changes the buying motion in three ways:

1. **The customer of yard efficiency is the brand owner, not FedEx itself.** When dwell time goes down, FedEx wins because (a) they can take more volume per door, (b) their site SLAs improve, and (c) they reduce detention exposure on customer-controlled carrier mix
2. **Multi-tenant facilities mean multi-tenant yards.** Memphis (1.1M sq ft) and Milton, ON (400K sq ft) are confirmed multi-tenant — multiple brand owners' inventory in one site, multiple carrier mixes through one gate. Yard complexity is materially higher than a single-shipper site
3. **Returns volume = inbound chaos.** 475M returns/year flowing back through ~130 sites means inbound trailer volumes that don't match outbound patterns. Reverse logistics yards behave differently than forward DC yards — more variance in trailer arrival, more inspection time at the dock, more triage decisions before put-away

### Footprint Math
The 5,000+-facility figure in the modex-gtm dataset for FedEx is the **parent-level FedEx footprint** (Express stations, Ground stations, Freight terminals, Supply Chain warehouses combined). **Scott's controllable footprint is ~130 warehouses** — much smaller, but each one is a multi-shift, multi-customer, multi-carrier operation with active gate-in/dock-in patterns. Don't pitch him on 5,000 facilities. Pitch him on 130, with a sharp focus on the largest 20–30 multi-tenant sites where yard complexity is concentrated.

## 3. Current State, Initiatives & Pain Points

### The Nimble Alliance (Sep 2024) — Strategic Signal
FedEx Supply Chain announced a **strategic alliance and equity investment in Nimble**, an AI robotics fulfillment company that closed a $106M Series C at a $1B valuation. Scott personally fronted the announcement:

> "Nimble's cutting-edge AI robotics and autonomous fulfillment systems will help streamline operations for our team members and unlock new opportunities for our customers."

**What this signals for YardFlow:**
- Scott is **actively buying outside technology** rather than building internally — the buying door is open
- The investment thesis is **inside-the-warehouse fulfillment automation**, not outside-the-warehouse yard execution. The yard remains a gap
- Nimble is a **hardware-heavy capex play** despite Scott's earlier "you can't spend 15 million bucks" comment — meaning when the ROI is clear enough, he overrides his own austerity rule
- **YardFlow's pitch is the inverse of Nimble**: software-only, multi-site rollout, weeks-to-payback. The argument writes itself: "You found the inside-the-four-walls answer with Nimble. Here's the outside-the-four-walls equivalent."

### FedEx Corporate Restructuring — DRIVE & Network 2.0
FedEx Corp is in the middle of the largest structural cost takeout in its history. Two programs run concurrently:
- **DRIVE:** $4.0B in permanent cost reductions delivered in FY2025 vs. FY2023 baseline; targeting another $1B in FY2026
- **Network 2.0:** Closing 475+ stations through 2027 (~30% facility footprint reduction in the ground/parcel network); 65% of eligible volume expected to flow through consolidated network by FY2026 peak

**Important nuance:** DRIVE and Network 2.0 are **parcel-network programs**, not Supply Chain programs. Supply Chain is a separate segment. But the **cultural pressure** is universal — every business unit head at FedEx is operating under "find structural cost takeout, prove permanence" pressure. Scott's organization is not exempt. Any pitch that lands as "we cut measurable cost per trailer turn at facility level" reads aligned with the parent-company narrative he must report into.

### FedEx Freight Spinoff — June 1, 2026
FedEx Freight separates June 1, 2026 as standalone public company **FDXF**. Filed Form 10 on January 16, 2026. **Supply Chain stays inside FedEx Corp.** This matters because:
- Post-spin FedEx Corp is **more concentrated in Express + Ground + Supply Chain**
- Supply Chain becomes a **bigger relative bet** in the remaining portfolio
- Scott's strategic visibility inside the C-suite goes up. He will be under more pressure to **show growth and margin discipline** as one of three remaining segment heads
- MODEX 2026 (March 16–20, 2026) sat right in the middle of the spin runway. Scott was likely highly visible at the show as part of the corporate narrative repositioning

### Customer Wins That Stretch the Yard
- **Best Buy** named FedEx primary national parcel carrier (2025) — though primarily an Express/Ground deal, Supply Chain handles BBY returns and reverse logistics. Volume implication for Supply Chain reverse-logistics yards is significant
- **Amazon large-package delivery deal** announced May 2025, fully onboarded by Q3 FY2026 — again, Express/Ground primarily, but the volume halo affects Supply Chain capacity planning
- **Healthcare $500M+ quality agreements (FY2024 + FY2025)** — healthcare is exactly the vertical where dock scheduling discipline matters most: temperature-controlled, chain-of-custody, FDA-traceable. Yard execution is not optional in this vertical

### Known and Likely Pain Points

**1. Multi-tenant yard chaos.** Memphis (1.1M sq ft) and Milton (400K sq ft) are publicly confirmed multi-tenant healthcare facilities. Multi-tenant means multiple customer carrier mixes converging on one gate, with no shared visibility across tenants. Each customer's TMS dispatches independently. The 3PL gate has to reconcile arrivals from N different planning horizons.

**2. Returns inbound variance.** 475M returns annually flowing back through reverse-logistics sites. Returns trailers don't arrive on tight appointment windows the way outbound shipments do — they arrive when the parcel network finishes a sortation cycle. This makes inbound dock scheduling materially harder than at a forward DC.

**3. Customer SLA exposure on dock-to-stock cycle time.** 3PL contracts typically include **dock-to-stock cycle time SLAs** — the customer measures how fast their inventory becomes salable after arriving at the FedEx site. Yard dwell is the silent first hour of that SLA clock. Every hour saved in the yard is an hour banked toward SLA performance.

**4. Detention chargeback complexity.** In a multi-tenant 3PL, **who pays detention when a trailer sits?** The customer? The carrier? FedEx? This is contractually messy and often litigated at QBR-level. Better yard data = cleaner detention attribution = fewer chargeback disputes.

**5. The labor-economics ceiling.** Scott has publicly said capex automation doesn't pencil for 3-year contract logistics terms. He has a structural appetite for **operating-leverage technology** (subscription, fast-payback, no large capex) that fits inside contract economics. YardFlow fits that mold; Nimble does not — yet he bought Nimble anyway, suggesting his real bar is "demonstrable per-site ROI" not "no capex"

**6. Heritage GENCO + heritage FedEx + heritage acquisitions integration.** ~130 sites built up over 30 years, with different WMS instances, different gate procedures, different yard practices at each. Network-wide standardization is a known, hard problem. The pitch: **YardFlow becomes the standardization layer above heterogeneous site infrastructure** — same gate-in/dock-in workflow at every site, regardless of what WMS or YMS lives behind it [unverified — the WMS heterogeneity is inferred from acquisition history, not publicly confirmed]

## 4. YardFlow Opportunity

### The Anchor Comparable — and Why It Translates
YardFlow's anchor customer is a **237-facility North American CPG network** that displaced an incumbent yard system and is now multi-year contracted on YardFlow. **Public comparable: Primo Brands (NYSE: PRMB)**, the largest bottled-water producer in North America, which YardFlow operates inside as a public reference.

The translation to FedEx Supply Chain is uncomfortably good:
- **Network scale:** 237 anchor sites vs. ~130 FedEx Supply Chain sites — same order of magnitude, both networks where per-site rollout economics matter
- **Multi-temperature, multi-mode complexity:** anchor has dry/cold/multi-zone; FedEx Supply Chain has ambient/temp-controlled/healthcare cold chain
- **Mixed carrier base:** anchor uses both private fleet and 3PL carriers; FedEx Supply Chain runs on customer-dispatched carriers (which is *more* heterogeneous than anchor)
- **Standardization-over-heterogeneity:** anchor needed one yard standard across decades of acquisitions; FedEx Supply Chain has the same problem at higher amplitude

### Why YardFlow Wins Here Specifically
1. **Software-only, no capex.** Lands inside Scott's stated "labor economics" framework. Per-site subscription model rolls inside the existing contract-logistics P&L without triggering capital approval cycles
2. **Multi-tenant gate workflow.** YardFlow's gate-in flow handles multiple shipper accounts at one site — a near-unique requirement in 3PL ops that most yard tools handle poorly. This is differentiated against PINC/Kaleris/FourKites, which were built for single-shipper deployments
3. **Returns-mode dock execution.** YardFlow's dock-in flow handles inbound triage, not just outbound load. Returns sites need this. Forward-DC-only YMS tools degrade in reverse-logistics environments
4. **Site-level ROI in 90 days.** Anchor customer's first-site ROI proof was under 90 days. Repeat that math at the largest 5–10 FedEx Supply Chain multi-tenant sites and the network rollout sells itself

### The Margin Math
Conservative model for FedEx Supply Chain at scale:
- 130 sites × average 30 trailer turns/day × 24-minute reduction in turn time = **~1,560 driver-hours/day saved** across the network
- At loaded driver/equipment cost of ~$60/hour, that's ~$94K/day = **~$34M/year in carrier-side detention exposure reduction**
- Even if FedEx captures only 20–30% of that (the rest accruing to carriers and brand-owner customers), that's **$7–10M/year in measurable margin contribution**, against a YardFlow subscription cost dramatically below that figure
- **Plus:** recovered dock door capacity reduces the need to add facilities or shift overtime — the second-order saving is larger than the first-order
- [unverified — figures are illustrative based on 24-min turn reduction observed at the anchor; actual FedEx baseline trailer turn time is not publicly disclosed]

Supply Chain doesn't have an analogous public program — but Scott does have the same internal pressure to show throughput-per-site improvement. **YardFlow gives Scott a Supply Chain analog of Network 2.0:** "Same 130 sites, materially more throughput per site, no facility additions, software-only investment."
