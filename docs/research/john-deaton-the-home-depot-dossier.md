## 2. The Home Depot Supply Chain — Operational Footprint

### Company Overview
- **Revenue:** **$164.7B** in fiscal 2025 (+3.2% YoY); comparable sales +0.3%
- **Net earnings:** $14.2B / $14.23 diluted EPS (FY2025)
- **Stores:** ~2,300+ in U.S., Canada, Mexico
- **Pro share:** 5% of customers, **~55% of sales** [per ontheseams.substack.com primer]
- **HQ:** Atlanta, GA (Store Support Center)

### Distribution Network (the core of Deaton's domain)

Per Supply Chain Dive (Dec 2025) and HD's own materials, the company has added **nearly 200 facilities over the past 8 years**. The network is unusually segmented by use case:

| Facility Type | Approx. Count | Size | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| Import Distribution Centers (IDCs) | 7 | 800K–1.5M sq ft | Port intake, route to RDC/SDC/BDC/FDC |
| Rapid Deployment Centers (RDCs) | ~19 | ~550K sq ft | High-turn store replenishment |
| Internal Freight Consolidators (IFCs) | 6 | Co-located with RDCs | Inbound supplier consolidation |
| Stocking Distribution Centers (SDCs) | Multiple | 500K–1.5M sq ft | Conveyable inventory holding |
| Bulk Distribution Centers (BDCs) | Multiple | 100K–300K sq ft | Non-conveyable bulk |
| **Flatbed Distribution Centers (FDCs)** | **17 open, ~40 planned** | ~800K sq ft | **Drive-through flatbed for Pro/jobsite** |
| Direct Fulfillment Centers (DFCs) | 19–20 | Variable | E-commerce; ~100K SKUs |
| Market Delivery Operations (MDOs) | **~160** | ~75K sq ft | Appliance + bulky last-mile cross-dock |

Source figures from Supply Chain Dive and the ontheseams.substack.com primer.

### What's Different About This Network
- **The FDC is a yard-management problem in the architecture itself.** Drive-through buildings designed for **65–75 flatbed trucks per day** at a single site are inherently dock-and-yard intensive. Lumber, roofing, drywall, and insulation are loaded from both sides while the trailer rolls through.
- **Pro flow is fragmenting away from stores.** Jobsite-direct deliveries from FDCs reduce store-yard congestion, but they concentrate trailer activity at the FDC node.
- **The "Relay" program (launched 2025).** Drivers pre-position loaded flatbed trailers in **store parking lots overnight**, dispatched the next morning to jobsites. Initial Atlanta test expanded into **18 incremental markets**. This is — quite literally — a yard-management product layered on top of store real estate.

### SRS + GMS: A New Distribution Subsidiary, ~1,050 More Sites
- **SRS Distribution acquired June 2024** for **~$18.25B EV** — HD's largest deal ever. SRS = ~750 branches across 47 states, 2,500 sales reps, **4,000 trucks** (a private fleet, in contrast to HD's mostly outsourced linehaul)
- **GMS acquired Sept 4, 2025** by SRS for **~$5.5B** — ~300 distribution centers + ~100 tool rental/service centers
- Combined SRS+GMS network: **~1,050+ specialty distribution branches**, mostly serving roofing, pool, landscaping, drywall, ceilings, and steel framing Pros
- This is a **second supply chain Deaton's organization now has to integrate or coordinate with** [unverified — exact reporting line for SRS post-acquisition is not publicly clear; SRS retains its CEO and is run as a subsidiary]

### Logistics Model
- Mix of contracted carriers and dedicated fleet for store replenishment
- SRS adds a **private flatbed fleet** of ~4,000 trucks — a very different operating model from core HD
- "**Ship from best location**" routing algorithm runs across the asset base — selects DC, MDO, or store as origin per order

## 4. Known Logistics Pain Points

### 1. The FDC dock-throughput ceiling
Each FDC is built for 65–75 flatbed turns/day. With 17 live and ~40 planned, the network is throughput-limited by **how fast trailers can be loaded, staged, and dispatched** — the literal definition of a yard problem. Every minute shaved off dock dwell at an FDC translates directly into either more loads/day or fewer trailers required to hit the same volume.

### 2. Pro complex orders are inherently multi-trailer, multi-stop
A roofing job needs shingles + underlayment + nails + flashing — potentially from FDC + SRS branch + GMS branch. Coordinating arrivals, dock assignments, and outbound staging across multiple trailers per project is the kind of scheduling complexity that breaks legacy yard processes.

### 3. The Relay program is a yard product without a yard system
Pre-positioning flatbed trailers in store parking lots overnight, then dispatching to jobsites the next morning, requires real-time visibility into:
- Which trailer is at which store
- What's loaded on it
- When it left, when it arrived, condition on arrival
- Driver handoff between linehaul and last-mile
This is a yard management use case running on what is, today, **store parking lot real estate** — not a purpose-built yard.

### 4. SRS/GMS integration: ~1,050 Pro distribution sites with their own systems
SRS and GMS each came with their own WMS, dispatching, and driver management. Knitting those into HD's network — even loosely — creates a federated yard-and-dock visibility problem at a scale most enterprises never face. [unverified — public detail on SRS/GMS systems integration plan is limited]

### 5. The throughput gap between automated upstream and labor-intensive yards
HD has spent years on **upstream automation and mechanization** (Deaton's own framing in 2023). But same-day/next-day commitments depend on the **boundary between TMS and WMS** — the yard. Faster loading inside the building doesn't help if trailers can't be sequenced to the right door at the right time.

### 6. Carrier experience in a partially-outsourced fleet
HD uses contract carriers heavily for store replenishment and inbound. SRS brings 4,000 owned trucks. **Driver wait time at HD facilities is a carrier-procurement variable** — in a tight market, HD competes with Walmart, Lowe's, Target, and the parcel networks for capacity. Yards that respect driver time win.

### 7. Capex plateau + market softness = throughput-per-asset is the new game
With "essentially completed" network buildout and FY26 sales growth guided to 2.5–4.5%, Deaton's organization can no longer expect its way out of bottlenecks by opening more buildings. The next decade of supply chain wins at HD will be **squeezing more out of the existing footprint**.

## 6. Key Insight: What Makes John Deaton Care About Yard Management?

### Primary Hook: The "essentially complete" pivot
HD told investors in Dec 2025 that the FDC/MDO/DFC buildout is essentially done. **The next 3 years of supply chain ROI come from utilization, not construction.** Yard and dock optimization is the canonical "more from what you have" play. Deaton's mandate has visibly shifted from **build** to **run hot**.

### The FDC is a flatbed yard, by design
HD spent years and ~$1.2B+ designing buildings explicitly for flatbed throughput. The FDC value proposition (65–75 flatbeds/day, drive-through, both-sides loading) is fundamentally a **dock-and-yard product**. Anything that compresses the yard cycle at an FDC compounds across the network.

### Relay needs a system
Pre-staging flatbed trailers at store parking lots overnight is operationally elegant but software-thin. As Relay scales beyond 18 markets, the operational complexity of **trailer-to-store-to-jobsite handoffs** gets harder, not easier.

### The Pro share-of-wallet thesis depends on reliability
HD's 2026 strategic story is winning the Pro across the entire project. Pros don't tolerate wrong-trailer, wrong-time, wrong-yard delivery. **Reliability is the Pro currency** — and it's predominantly a yard execution problem.

### SRS/GMS federation is unfinished business
1,050+ specialty distribution branches now sit inside HD's enterprise. The natural next move is selective tech standardization — and yard/dock visibility is the kind of light-footprint layer that can ship across heterogeneous branches without ripping out underlying WMS.

### Cost discipline is non-negotiable
Deaton owned the $500M savings program and delivered. With FY26 EPS guided to **flat to +4%** in a soft housing market, his org is again expected to find continuous productivity. Yard turn-time savings convert directly into trucking labor and demurrage line items.

### Sustainability and idle time
Truck idle time at facilities is a measurable Scope 3 contributor. HD reports on emissions; reducing yard dwell across an FDC network handling tens of thousands of flatbeds/year is a concrete, defensible ESG line item.
