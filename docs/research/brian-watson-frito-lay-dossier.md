## 2. Frito-Lay Supply Chain — Operational Footprint

### Company Overview
- **Frito-Lay North America (FLNA)** = PepsiCo's North American snacks division: Lay's, Doritos, Cheetos, Tostitos, Ruffles, Sun Chips, Fritos, Stacy's, SmartFood, plus the newly acquired Siete portfolio
- Parent (PepsiCo) revenue: **~$92B** (FY2024); FLNA is the largest single profit contributor
- HQ: Plano, TX; reports into PepsiCo Foods North America (Steven Williams, CEO)

### Manufacturing & Distribution Footprint
- **30+ manufacturing plants** across the U.S. and Canada
- **200+ distribution centers** — this is the number that matters most for yard work; DCs, not factories, are where DSD trailer churn lives
- **~15,000 DSD routes** running out of those DCs
- **~500,000 weekly retail-store visits** to **~315,000 retail customers** — the largest direct-store-delivery network in North America by an order of magnitude
- Modesto, CA is the published flagship plant: 500,000 sq ft, 80 acres, 1,100+ associates, the showcase for PepsiCo's zero/near-zero emissions playbook

### Logistics Model — The Critical Difference vs. General Mills
Frito-Lay does **not** look like a typical 3PL-dependent CPG shipper. The operating model is:
- **Plant → Frito-Lay-operated DC → Frito-Lay-operated DSD route truck → retailer shelf**
- Frito-Lay runs its own bobtails and route trucks; the company is in the process of electrifying that fleet (Modesto, Memphis with Einride, expanded EV deployments through 2025)
- Long-haul plant-to-DC moves use a mix of dedicated/contracted carriers (Schneider EV partnership confirmed) and PepsiCo's own assets
- **The yard problem is therefore Frito-Lay's own problem.** They are the carrier, the shipper, the receiver, and the gate guard at the same site. There is no 3PL to push dwell costs onto. Every minute of yard delay is a minute Frito-Lay pays for, twice.

### Supply Chain Scope Relevant to Brian's Patch
For the Siete Transformation specifically:
- Siete's products (grain-free tortillas, chips, salsas, cookies) need to be slotted into Frito-Lay's existing DC and DSD network
- Different production sourcing (co-mans, contract manufacturing) → inbound trailers from new origin points hitting DCs that weren't designed to receive them
- This is the specific seam where **inbound dock scheduling and yard visibility break first** — when an integration adds new carriers and new SKU profiles to a yard tuned for a steady-state network

## 4. Known Logistics Pain Points

### 1. Concentration into Surviving DCs (Right Now)
The pattern from the closures is unambiguous: manufacturing is leaving certain sites, **but DC and transportation operations stay**. Rancho Cucamonga is the textbook case — plant gone, yard volume preserved or increased. Surviving DCs are eating throughput from closed sister sites in 2025–2026. This is the YardFlow timing window.

### 2. The DSD Yard is a Different Animal
Most of the YardFlow account universe runs traditional shipper yards: long-haul trucks in, long-haul trucks out, dock turn measured in tens of minutes per drop. **Frito-Lay's DCs run a DSD pattern:** dozens of route trucks staged for early-morning dispatch, returning empty mid-day, reloaded and re-dispatched. The yard is closer to an airline hub than a traditional CPG dock — every minute of route-truck delay cascades into late-store-visits, missed shelf windows, and lost sales. **Standard yard-management products tuned for OTR shipper flows under-serve this pattern.** YardFlow's gate-in → dock-in standardization is exactly the layer DSD operators need but rarely get from incumbents.

### 3. Siete Integration = New Inbound Profile at Existing DCs
Brian's specific mandate. Pre-acquisition Siete had its own contract manufacturers, its own carriers, its own delivery rhythms. Post-acquisition, those flows are being routed into Frito-Lay DCs that were designed around a different, more uniform Frito-Lay-native inbound pattern. **New carriers + new SKU profiles + same yard = inbound dock contention.** This is a textbook moment for inbound appointment scheduling and yard visibility tooling.

### 4. SKU Rationalization Tail Risk
Cutting 35% of FLNA SKUs sounds like a yard simplification. It also creates an 18-month period of mixed-fleet, mixed-pack-format complexity as the legacy SKUs phase out, the new lineup phases in, and DCs run both at once. SKU transitions are a documented yard-chaos trigger across CPG.

### 5. EV Fleet Charging Logistics
Frito-Lay's EV trucks introduce a **new yard problem**: charge windows. An ICE bobtail can sit in a yard for hours at zero operational cost. An EV bobtail in a charge stall is occupying a charging slot — a finite, capital-constrained resource. **Yard slot scheduling for EVs is a real and underserved problem.** YardFlow gate-to-dock orchestration that treats charge stalls as first-class yard resources is the obvious extension, and one of the few categories of CPG yard work where the buyer's own publicly-committed sustainability goals mandate solving it.

### 6. Public Carrier-Relationship Pressure
Schneider, Einride, and the broader carrier base are publicly partnered with Frito-Lay on emissions and EV pilots. These partnerships only continue if Frito-Lay yards are credible places to operate. Carrier dwell complaints in this segment are reputation-dangerous, not just cost-dangerous.

### 7. The Transportation Control Tower / Manufacturing Control Tower Seam
PepsiCo has publicly disclosed a "supply chain hub" architecture with a **Manufacturing Control Tower (MCT)** and **Transportation Control Tower (TCT)** running on top of SAP S/4HANA. The MCT sees the plant. The TCT sees the in-transit lane. **Neither tower sees the yard.** The gate-in → dock-in window is the single largest data gap in the published architecture, and the only one that materially affects both production scheduling (when does the trailer of inputs actually clear the gate) and transportation execution (when does the outbound load actually depart). YardFlow inserts cleanly here as the missing tower-segment, not as a competitor to either MCT or TCT.
