## 5. Known Logistics Pain Points

### 1. Mixed-Fleet Yard at Every Plant
Aerosols (hazmat) + ambient liquids + Ziploc (cube-extreme) + professional-channel (B2B) all flow through the same plants and the same RDCs. **No two trailers in the yard have the same constraints.** Per-site YMS tools handle this with site-specific rules; what gets lost is **network-level standardization across the mix.** Camacho's Reckitt and Danone tours both had simpler product mixes than SC Johnson's — this is structurally his hardest network.

### 2. Waxdale Is Singular and Vulnerable
2.2M sq ft, 60M cases/year, 15 production lines, the world's fastest aerosol site. **One plant carries an outsized fraction of the brand portfolio.** Yard disruption at Waxdale ripples through Glade, Raid, Off!, Pledge, Scrubbing Bubbles, and Windex simultaneously. The criticality concentration here is much higher than at a more-distributed network like Kraft Heinz or General Mills.

### 3. RDC Network Pressure from Mass Retail
SC Johnson's outbound customers are the same mass-retail concentrators as every other CPG: Walmart, Target, Costco, Sam's, Kroger, Amazon. **MABD penalties at these accounts have tightened materially through 2024–2025.** Yard delays at the outbound RDC translate directly to late retail deliveries → chargebacks → customer-scorecard reports. This is not a softening pressure point.

### 4. Hazmat Routing Across the Network
Aerosols and certain chemical SKUs are DOT-regulated for transport. **Hazmat-classified trailers have specific routing, parking, and dock-door rules** that constrain yard flexibility in ways non-hazmat networks don't experience. Site-level yard tools generally handle this; **network-level orchestration of hazmat-plus-non-hazmat trailer mixes across multiple plants and RDCs is rare in available tooling.**

### 5. Carrier Relationship Pressure in Cube-Heavy and Hazmat-Heavy Lanes
SC Johnson's trailer count is disproportionately high for its tonnage (Ziploc cube-out) and disproportionately constrained for its routing (aerosol hazmat rules). **Carriers running SC Johnson lanes earn less per cube-mile and face more routing restrictions** than carriers running easier CPG freight. In tight capacity markets, carriers re-prioritize. Yard-friction reduction is a measurable carrier-procurement lever — and that lever lands at the Camacho–Westfahl seam.

### 6. Brantford Consolidation = Yard Pressure on Surviving Canadian Sites
The publicly-announced "combine existing centers in Canada" framing for Brantford is the canonical consolidation pattern: same demand, fewer DCs, more trailers per surviving yard. This is happening now.

### 7. Private-Company Capex Discipline
SC Johnson, while long-horizon, is not loose with capex. The conservatism of family-ownership capital discipline means **yard improvements that require new pavement, new towers, new gates, or new automation hardware will face a steep approval gradient.** Pure-software / network-orchestration improvements that lift throughput at existing yards without capex have a meaningfully shorter approval path.

### 8. Sustainability Reporting Granularity
Fisk Johnson personally champions plastic regulation and EPR; SC Johnson publishes detailed sustainability reports. **Yard idling hours, hostler diesel usage, and trailer-staging Scope 1 emissions are not currently captured at the granularity Fisk's external advocacy implies they should be.** This is an unforced gap and worth noting.
