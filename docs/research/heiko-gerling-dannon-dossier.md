## 4. Known Logistics Pain Points (The Dannon-Specific View)

### 1. Multi-Temperature Dock Contention at Multi-Category Facilities

Danone NA is unusual in the CPG world because at a single facility, multiple temperature-zone products may share dock surface. At Mount Crawford alone: **Silk (refrigerated plant-based), Horizon-era assets (chilled), International Delight (refrigerated creamer), STōK (refrigerated coffee), So Delicious (frozen and refrigerated)** all flow through the same yard. Trailers carrying different temperature-zone products **compete for the same dock doors**, and a misassignment is a quality event, not just a scheduling annoyance.

### 2. The 14-Day Fresh Dairy Shelf Clock

Fresh yogurt has roughly a 14-day shelf life from production. Every minute of yard dwell is a minute off the customer's at-shelf life. **At dairy margins, that compounds into shrink, service recovery, and retailer chargebacks.** This pain is concentrated at the yogurt plants (Minster, Fort Worth) but cascades through the DC network when fresh-yogurt and longer-shelf product share regional warehousing.

### 3. Volume Surge Without Dock Surface Expansion

The Minster expansion adds production capacity but the announced footprint is roughly +48,000 sq ft of plant, not a doubled dock count. **60% more milk in equals proportionally more finished goods out — through largely the same dock surface.** This is the General Mills consolidation pattern in miniature: more throughput, same docks, yard becomes the constraint.

### 4. New Jacksonville DC Ramp-Up

The new Southeast distribution hub being built around Jacksonville is a **net-new node**. New facilities typically run 15–25% below optimal dock utilization in their first 12–18 months as standard operating procedures get worked out. This is the perfect window to embed a yard standard before bad habits set.

### 5. Imported Premium Water — Different Logistics DNA

evian and Volvic are **imported from France** (Évian-les-Bains and Volvic, respectively). Different inbound port operations, different palletization, different drayage carriers, different appointment systems than the dairy network. When evian volume runs through the same regional DCs as fresh dairy, it's running on a different yard playbook by definition. [unverified — DC-level commingling is plausible but not publicly documented]

### 6. 13-Plant Fragmentation, Each With Local Yard Practice

Public materials and Danone NA's own facility-by-facility press releases imply **each plant runs its own gate, dock, and spotter routine**. There's no public reference to a centralized YMS. The White Plains supply chain team trying to see a network view of trailer utilization is — as the existing Dannon memo posits — stitching together inputs from 13+ local teams. This is a fragmentation pattern, not an automation pattern; it's the gap YardFlow's `flowNETWORK` is designed for.

### 7. Carrier Detention Across Fresh Network

Fresh dairy carriers operate on tight appointment windows with low tolerance for waste. **In a fresh network, "treat my driver well" is non-negotiable for capacity assurance** — because the alternative carriers (in regional refrigerated) are limited and have pricing power. Detention costs at fresh-product plants compound faster than at dry CPG networks.

### 8. Co-Manufacturing Coordination

Roughly **9 of the 22 production points** in Danone NA's network are contract manufacturers. Yard operations at co-mans are even more fragmented — each co-man runs its own protocol. Standardizing inbound/outbound visibility across owned + contract sites is the holy grail and the hardest piece. [unverified — exact co-man yard architecture not publicly documented]
