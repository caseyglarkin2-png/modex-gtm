## 2. Barnes & Noble Supply Chain — Operational Footprint

### Company Overview
- **Revenue:** ~$3B combined (B&N + Waterstones under Elliott ownership, per Dec 2025 reporting)
- **Profit:** ~$400M annual on the combined entity
- **Stores:** 702 B&N outlets at end of 2025 (includes 9 from the Books Inc. acquisition); growing 60+/year toward a stated **1,000-store target**
- **Owner:** Elliott Investment Management (private since 2019); reportedly preparing a B&N + Waterstones IPO in 2H 2026
- **CEO:** James Daunt (also CEO of Waterstones)

### Distribution Center Footprint (the Whole Network)
B&N runs a **2-DC** national network — radically smaller than the CPG accounts in Wave 1, but doing more throughput per facility than almost any specialty retailer:

| Facility | Size | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Monroe Township, NJ** (1 Barnes & Noble Way) | **1.15M sq ft** on 73 acres, 115 exterior doors, 675-car parking | East-coast stores + national e-commerce fulfillment hub |
| **Reno, NV** (12660 Old Virginia Rd, Damonte Ranch) | **~601K sq ft** | West-coast store replenishment; lease renewed November 2025 [unverified — term length not in public filings] |

**Door count is the load-bearing number.** Monroe Township's 115 exterior doors is unusual for a 1.15M-sq-ft retail DC and signals very high inbound publisher-trailer cadence. This is a 2-DC network punching at 4-DC volumes, which is exactly the kind of footprint where yard congestion compounds the fastest.

### Inbound Profile (What Hits Their Yards)
- **Heavy inbound from publisher DCs:** PRH (Westminster MD, Crawfordsville IN, Hampstead MD), Hachette, HarperCollins, Macmillan, Simon & Schuster, Scholastic, plus academic/university press carriers
- **Bills of lading filed:** ~17,000 import manifests filed by B&N Distribution Inc. between Oct 2012 and Jan 2026 — meaningful international inbound (UK and global publishers shipping to Monroe Township)
- **3PL transportation:** B&N uses **Jarrett Logistics Systems (JLS)** as TMS / carrier-management partner per their published vendor guidelines — they are not a private fleet operator
- **Outbound:** Store-replenishment loads to 700+ retail outlets + e-commerce parcel out of Monroe (UPS / USPS / FedEx)

### Tech Stack (What's Public, What's Inferred)
- **TMS:** Jarrett Logistics Systems (confirmed in public vendor guidelines)
- **Material handling at Monroe:** Century Conveyor Systems integrated the carton/tote materials handling and a single-loop tilt-tray sorter rated to **170 cartons/min** — installed when the DC was completed in 2004. That generation of automation is now ~22 years old. [unverified whether B&N has retrofitted since]
- **Order management / WMS:** B&N has been actively hiring "E-commerce Fulfillment Engineer" roles for Monroe Township to "support, upgrade and enhance their existing order management solution" — implies legacy OMS being modernized rather than a greenfield platform
- **No publicly known YMS** — Annette's own podcast comments about distribution-side automation being "underinvested compared to manufacturing" suggest she sees this gap herself

### Returns / Reverse Logistics Transformation
The single most-discussed B&N supply-chain story of the Daunt era is the **returns rate going from ~30% to ~7%** by 2024 — a dramatic structural change driven by killing publisher-paid placement and pushing inventory autonomy to individual stores. Reverse logistics volume is permanently lower at the DCs than it was pre-2019, but **forward volume is climbing** with 60+ new stores per year and TikTok-driven demand spikes (romantasy, BookTok). The yard load is shifting from inbound-returns-heavy to inbound-replenishment-heavy at a network with no slack.

## 3. Current State, Initiatives, and Pain Points

### Macro Initiatives Annette is Walking Into
1. **Open 60+ stores in 2026** (same pace as 2025); each new store needs replenishment volume from the existing 2-DC network. Network throughput must rise meaningfully without DC count rising.
2. **IPO preparation (Elliott / 2H 2026)** — supply chain becomes part of the equity story. Public-company comps will benchmark B&N's logistics cost ratio; CFO and CEO will want supply-chain narrative beats that read well to institutional investors.
3. **Books Inc. acquisition integration (late 2025)** — 9 added stores, plus B&N is reportedly buying additional indie chains. Each acquired store needs to integrate into the same DC network.
4. **TikTok / viral-demand responsiveness** — the same store-level autonomy that fixed returns now creates wildly variable replenishment patterns. Reno and Monroe see surge orders that did not exist in the Borders era.
5. **OMS / fulfillment software modernization** — actively hiring fulfillment engineers in Monroe, suggesting an in-progress upgrade cycle.

### AI / Tech Stance
Annette is the most explicitly **automation-curious** CSCO in this batch of dossiers. From her podcast and Bradley profile:
- She names "automation, both physical and process-based" as the dominant trend, and specifically points out distribution and fulfillment as **underinvested relative to manufacturing**
- She emphasizes Power BI / Excel / SIPOC fluency — she is data-literate but not a "build me an AI lab" CSCO. She wants tools her teams can operate.
- Her TOC / buffer-management background means she is mathematically allergic to point solutions that don't address the system constraint. **A pitch that doesn't identify the constraint will lose her in 90 seconds.**

### Pain Points — Specific to This Account
1. **2 DCs serving 700+ stores growing to 1,000.** Annette can't add a third DC on Elliott's IPO timeline. Throughput per yard must rise.
2. **Monroe Township is e-commerce + east-coast retail + international inbound concentrated in one yard.** 115 doors sounds like a lot until you imagine inbound publisher trailers, returns containers, store-replen outbound, and parcel pickups all competing for them.
3. **Inbound publisher cadence is highly bursty** — fall list, spring list, holiday, BookTok-driven reorders. Yard scheduling at Monroe is a known quarterly headache for any book DC operator.
4. **22-year-old DC infrastructure** (Monroe 2004 build) means yard ops likely look closer to 2010 than 2026. Fresh CSCO + IPO timeline = window for capex justification.
5. **Carrier relationships in a tight specialty-retail market** — when JLS calls drivers for B&N loads, driver wait time at Monroe is a competitive liability.
6. **Reno lease was just renewed (Nov 2025)** — they are committed to this 2-DC footprint for the medium term. They have to make these two yards do more.

### What She is *Not* Going to Care About
- Generic "AI for supply chain" pitches with no constraint thesis
- Multi-year transformation programs that don't show value before the IPO window
- Anything that requires changing the JLS-managed carrier model
- Systems pitched without consideration for how they integrate to Power BI / existing reporting

## 4. YardFlow Opportunity

### Why This Account is Real, Despite the Small Footprint
Most Wave-1 accounts are 30+ DC CPG networks. B&N is **2 DCs**. So why is this Wave 1?

1. **Annette personally**, not B&N as an account, is the wedge. She sits on the MHI Board of Governors, is a MODEX 2026 panelist, has 30 years of network in publishing AND retail supply chain, and is explicitly on record saying distribution-side automation is underinvested. **She is a network multiplier** — every CSCO conversation she has in Atlanta touches our category.
2. **The 2-DC constraint is the pitch.** When you cannot add facilities, every minute of yard dwell costs more. The math at Monroe Township (1.15M sq ft, 115 doors, e-commerce + retail + international inbound) is the math we are best at.
3. **IPO window.** Elliott wants a clean supply-chain story for 2H 2026. Yard-execution data feeding into operating-cost narrative is exactly the kind of metric the equity story benefits from.
4. **Retail specialty is a beachhead category.** If we can land B&N (or even just earn Annette's "have you talked to X" intro), we open a door to Waterstones, Books Inc., and adjacent Elliott portfolio companies.

### The Specific Angle for Annette
**Frame YardFlow as the "process automation" half of the automation story she has already publicly named as underinvested.**

She isn't going to be wowed by "we save 24 minutes of dwell." She's going to ask:
- *What's the constraint?*
- *Where's the buffer?*
- *How does this read in Power BI to my team?*
- *Does it depend on changing my JLS relationship?* (answer: no)

The pitch that lands with her is **not** about the truck-turn metric. It's about **the system constraint at a 2-DC retail network being inbound-yard scheduling**, and YardFlow being the buffer-management layer between TMS-in-transit and WMS-in-warehouse. She will hear that thesis and recognize her own framework in it.

### The Comparable
**Do not** lead with the Tier-1 CPG anchor (237-facility network). The scale gap is too wide and the use-case (multi-temp food manufacturing) is too far from books. Lead with **Primo Brands** as the public comparable — a multi-DC network where yard discipline is the unlock for replenishment density. Use the anchor only as a "we have done this at network-scale" credibility flex if she presses for proof.

### Talking Points (Ranked)
1. **"You called it on the podcast — distribution and fulfillment automation is underinvested."** Open with her own language. She'll engage immediately.
2. **"Two DCs, 700 stores going to 1,000, and an IPO in 18 months. The yard is the constraint."** TOC framing she will recognize.
3. **"We integrate with Jarrett, not around them."** Pre-empt the question.
4. **"The Monroe yard is doing what a 4-DC network's yards normally do. We see the same shape at [public-comparable Primo Brands] and at a 237-facility CPG network we run."**
5. **"Power BI feeds. Your team operates this in their existing dashboards."** Match her tooling preference.
6. **"22-year-old DC infrastructure, fresh CSCO, IPO window — this is the timing question, not the technology question."**

### Watch Out For
- **PINC/Kaleris:** confirm whether they have already pitched her. She has known the YMS category for 15 years; she will have heard that pitch. We need to come in as the network/process-automation alternative, not as YMS-#3.
- **Her industrial-engineering rigor.** She will ask precise questions about queueing model, dock-door utilization curves, and how we handle inbound vs outbound prioritization. Bring an engineer.
