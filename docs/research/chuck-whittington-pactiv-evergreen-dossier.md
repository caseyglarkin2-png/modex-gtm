## 4. Known Logistics Pain Points (The Pactiv Evergreen + Novolex-Specific View)

### 1. PINC Solved 2019. The Combined Network Is a 2025+ Problem.

The PINC case study documenting Pactiv's coast-to-coast YMS deployment at 12 facilities was published **October 2019**. That deployment solved the *site-level* yard problem at *Pactiv* as it existed in 2019:

- 200–300 trailers in yard at any given time at named sites
- Spotters spending **15–20 minutes** searching for specific trailers
- GPS-based trailer location + check-in-by-loop spotter model

**That problem is solved.** The 2026 problem is different in shape entirely:

- **~95+ combined Novolex+Pactiv yard surfaces** (up from ~50–60 Pactiv-only)
- **Two parallel operating models** — Pactiv sites on PINC, Novolex sites on ? — and no unified network view
- **39,000 SKUs** moving across the combined network, vs the much smaller 2019 Pactiv-only SKU set
- **Integration team needs a single dashboard view of yard state across the combined footprint** — PINC was not built for that, and adding more PINC instances doesn't solve it.

This is **the displacement story for this account, specifically.**

### 2. Mixing Center Misroutes Compound Under Combined-Network Complexity

Temple, TX (publicly named) and other regional mixing centers blend formulations from manufacturing and distribute to downstream nodes. **The same trailer often serves multiple downstream destinations.** A yard misroute at a mixing center cascades: the trailer that should have left at 10 AM for downstream node A leaves at 11:30 AM, missing the appointment window at node A *and* delaying the appointment at downstream node B.

When the mixing center surface grows from "Pactiv brands only" to "Pactiv + Novolex combined brands," **the misroute risk grows non-linearly with SKU count.** Site-level PINC at the mixing center can identify where a trailer *is*. It cannot orchestrate *which trailer should leave next* across a unified combined-network plan.

### 3. Foodservice Customer Service Level = Zero Tolerance for Yard Delay

McDonald's and Starbucks are *the* archetypal JIT foodservice customers. Their distribution networks expect predictable, on-time inbound shipments with **zero stockouts and zero chargebacks.** Pactiv Evergreen's yard surface — between gate-in and dock-load — is the choke point that determines whether those service levels hold across the combined network.

**A 30-minute average yard dwell improvement is not a feature pitch. It is service-level insurance for the customer relationships that justify the combined company's largest revenue lines.**

### 4. Footprint Optimization = Volume Concentration at Remaining Sites

The Feb 2024 Pactiv Footprint Optimization plan, plus the Beverage Merchandising restructuring (2023), plus Kalamazoo (April 2025), plus the unannounced Novolex-side site rationalizations that will inevitably follow the combination — **collectively concentrate volume at the surviving sites.** The same yards see more trucks, more SKU diversity, more JIT pressure. **This is the General Mills consolidation pattern (and the Nestlé Solon consolidation pattern) — repeated at Pactiv Evergreen scale.**

### 5. Aberdeen NC and the PINC Job Posting Signal

The hunt list note flags an **NC job post referencing PINC** — most likely tied to Aberdeen NC operations. **What this tells us specifically:**

- PINC is *currently in use* at the Aberdeen site (the job post wouldn't reference it if it weren't a current operating system)

- A current PINC user at the working-operator level *will* be involved in any YardFlow evaluation. The pitch needs to **not antagonize the working-level PINC user** — frame the displacement as operating-model evolution, not as a referendum on the operator's existing tools.

### 6. Two Yard Stacks, No Network View, $6.7B in Synergy Pressure

This is the single most important operational read: **the integration team has a fiduciary obligation to deliver synergies to Apollo and CPP Investments.** Running two parallel yard stacks indefinitely is the opposite of synergy. The combined company has to either:

- **(a) Extend PINC to the Novolex-legacy sites** — possible, expensive, slow, and doesn't solve the network-view problem
- **(b) Migrate Pactiv-legacy sites to whatever Novolex has** — likely worse, given PINC's documented deployment depth on the Pactiv side
- **(c) Adopt a network-level orchestration layer that ingests from PINC at Pactiv sites, ingests from Novolex's stack at Novolex sites, and presents a single combined operating view** — *this is the YardFlow shape*

**Option (c) is the only path that doesn't waste the existing PINC investment on the Pactiv side while still delivering a combined-network view.** That's an unusual sales position: not displacing PINC at every site, but rendering PINC into one input feed of a larger network operating layer.

### 7. The Apollo / CPP Pressure Cadence

Private-equity-backed integrations run on aggressive synergy timelines. Apollo's underwriting model for the $6.7B deal assumes meaningful operating cost takeout by 2027. **Yard inefficiency is exactly the kind of "soft" operating cost line that integration teams target in months 12–24 post-close** — which means the window for Chuck's CTO seat to evaluate, commit, and deploy a network yard operating model is roughly **April 2025 through April 2027**. **We are currently in month 13 of that 24-month window.**

### 8. The Foodservice + Retail Packaging Multi-Channel Yard

Pactiv Evergreen's customer mix is split between **foodservice** (McDonald's, Starbucks, Sysco, US Foods, PFG) and **retail packaging** (grocery, club). Each channel has different appointment systems, different chargeback structures, different SKU velocity profiles. When trailers serving McDonald's compete for the same yard surface as trailers serving Kroger or club-store DCs, **the yard layer needs to distinguish by channel SLA, not just by trailer presence.** PINC at the site level was not built to do this natively.
