## 3. The $5.5B Nava Expansion — Why This Changes Everything

### The Mega-Investment
- Constellation has invested **over $5.5 billion since 2013** in its Mexico beer operations, primarily at the Nava brewery
- This is one of the **largest single-company manufacturing investments in Mexico's history**
- The Nava brewery has been continuously expanded from its original ~10M hectoliter capacity to 35M+ hectoliters
- A **new Veracruz brewery** is under construction (announced ~2023-2024), adding another ~15M hectoliters

### Why It Matters for Yard Management

**The math is simple:**
- All beer is brewed in Mexico → shipped to US → received at US yards → distributed to retailers
- As capacity grows 50-70% with Veracruz coming online, **US receiving infrastructure must absorb proportionally more inbound volume**
- Every additional million hectoliters = thousands more truckloads crossing the border and hitting US receiving yards
- If yard throughput doesn't scale with brewing capacity, the entire $5.5B investment gets bottlenecked at the last mile

### Cross-Border Logistics Complexity
- Beer crosses the border via **rail (primarily) and truck** through CBP-regulated ports of entry
- Customs clearance, FDA holds (beverage alcohol requires TTB compliance), and border congestion create unpredictable arrival patterns
- Trailers arrive at US receiving yards in **waves** — not smooth, predictable flows
- **Summer beer season** (May-September) creates massive demand spikes — Corona and Modelo sell 40-60% more in summer months
- This means yard congestion peaks precisely when every minute of dock time matters most

## 4. Known Logistics Pain Points — The YardFlow Opportunity

### 4a. Cross-Border Receiving Chaos
- Trucks crossing from Mexico arrive on **border time, not appointment time** — CBP delays, bridge congestion, and broker holds mean arrival windows are wide
- US receiving yards must absorb unpredictable surges of inbound trailers
- If dock doors are occupied and yard staging is full, **trucks queue on public roads** — a DOT compliance issue and a driver satisfaction nightmare
- This is the exact problem YardFlow solves: dynamic yard orchestration that absorbs arrival variability

### 4b. Seasonal Demand Spikes
| Season | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| **Memorial Day → Labor Day** (beer) | 40-60% volume surge; Corona/Modelo peak consumption |
| **Cinco de Mayo** (beer) | Single biggest beer sales week of the year |
| **Thanksgiving → New Year's** (wine/spirits) | Wine and spirits peak; Kim Crawford, Robert Mondavi, High West spike |
| **Super Bowl** (beer) | Major promotional volume surge |
| **Spring Break** (beer) | Corona brand activation peak |

- Seasonal spikes mean the yard must handle **2-3x normal volume** for weeks at a time
- You don't build permanent infrastructure for peak — you need **software** that makes existing infrastructure flex

### 4c. Beverage Weight & Volume Challenges
- Beer is **heavy** (~2,200 lbs per pallet of 12-packs, ~20 pallets per trailer = ~44,000 lbs)
- Legal truck weight limits (80,000 lbs gross) mean beer trucks are often **weight-limited before they're cube-limited**
- This means **more trucks for the same volume** compared to lighter goods → more yard movements per unit of product
- Wine bottles are even heavier per unit — glass weight compounds the trailer count problem
- **More trailers = more yard moves = more dock appointments = more congestion**

### 4d. Three-Tier Distribution Complexity
- Constellation can't ship direct to retail — must go through licensed distributors
- This means product flows: **Mexico brewery → border crossing → Constellation receiving yard → Constellation DC → distributor pickup → distributor warehouse → retail**
- The Constellation yard is the **critical handoff point** where they control the flow before it enters the distributor network
- Any delay at the Constellation yard cascades to distributor fill rates → retailer out-of-stocks → lost sales

### 4e. Glass Plant Integration (Nava)
- The Owens-Illinois glass plant is **adjacent to the Nava brewery** — producing bottles on-site
- This creates **bidirectional yard traffic**: raw materials inbound + finished glass to brewery + finished beer outbound
- Complex yard choreography with multiple trailer types and priorities
