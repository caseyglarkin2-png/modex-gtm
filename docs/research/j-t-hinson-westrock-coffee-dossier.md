## 5. Known Logistics Pain Points

### 1. The 120 → 400 Trailer Scale Discontinuity
The Concord yard tripled. A YMS configured for a 120-trailer operation, even after seven years of incremental tuning, is not the same operating model as one designed for a 400-trailer operation. Site-grown configurations carry their original assumptions: yard zoning, exception handling, shared-queue routing logic, driver-journey thresholds. **Re-designing the operating model post-3× growth is the single highest-leverage yard-ops project this network has on its desk**.

### 2. Conway Is a Greenfield Yard With No Operating Model Yet
The Conway campus has three new buildings, 72 DC dock doors, two distinct manufacturing cadences (RTD canning, single-serve K-cup format), and ~900 employees at full capacity. The Kaleris deployment was born at Concord, not at Conway. **There is no entrenched yard-operating model at Conway to displace, and the design window for one is open right now.** Hinson's own frame — that the system needed *physical re-design* alongside *software adoption* — is the design conversation Conway needs.

### 3. The Three-Site Concord Footprint
Per Hinson's own description: main facility + remote yard (2 mi) + roasting plant (8 mi). Three sites that must coordinate trailer moves between them. The Kaleris queue solves intra-site visibility; **the inter-site choreography** (which trailer goes from yard to roaster to dock to dispatch on which schedule) is still operator-driven, not system-driven.

### 4. From Site-Level YMS to Network-Level Ops
Westrock now operates **three U.S. campuses** (Little Rock/N. Little Rock, Conway, Concord) plus the Malaysia facility. The site-level Kaleris deployment was born at Concord. There is no public reference to a network-level operating layer that arbitrates between Concord and Conway, or between coffee and RTD channel cadence. **This is the network-ops gap**.

### 5. Walmart-Grade OTIF Pressure With Same-Network Resources
*"Same resources, more output"* is the Inbound Logistics framing of Westrock's current operating gain. That framing only holds until the next customer-OTIF event. Lean-resource networks punish yard variance disproportionately — a 90-minute trailer delay used to be invisible to a major retail customer; in the current state it shows up on a vendor scorecard.

### 6. Co-Pack and Customer-Volume Diversity
The integrated-supplier strategy (coffee + tea + energy + protein, private label + foodservice + CPG branded) means more inbound packaging SKUs, more co-pack partner shipments, more outbound channel cadence. Each new customer relationship adds a freight pattern. **This is multi-cadence complexity, and multi-cadence complexity is where site-level YMS configurations age fastest**.

### 7. The Operating-Leverage Year Lens
CEO Scott Ford has externally committed to 2026 being the operating-leverage year. Operating leverage in a multi-plant fulfillment network is largely a *yard and dock* story — production variance is now a planning problem, not a capacity problem. The yard becomes the gate between "what plants produce" and "what customers receive."
