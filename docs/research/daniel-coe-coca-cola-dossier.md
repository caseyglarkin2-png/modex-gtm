## 2. The Coca-Cola Company Operational Footprint — A Critical Caveat

### TCCC Is Not A Bottler
This is the most important framing fact in this dossier. TCCC is the **brand owner and concentrate manufacturer**. Finished-goods bottling, warehousing, and DSD distribution in North America are run by **independent bottlers** — Coca-Cola Consolidated (the public one, KO-adjacent), Coca-Cola UNITED, Reyes Coca-Cola Bottling, Liberty Coca-Cola, Great Lakes Coca-Cola, etc. TCCC completed its **North American refranchising in October 2017**, exiting company-owned bottling entirely in the US.

Therefore, when YardFlow pitches Coe, we are NOT pitching the operator of the trailer-heavy bottling and DC yards. We are pitching the **system orchestrator** who can authorize a standard the bottlers then adopt through CEPG.

### What TCCC Itself Operates (Coe's Direct Footprint)
- **Concentrate plants:** A small number of company-owned facilities globally. US concentrate is centered in Atlanta (the Atlanta Syrup Plant) and a handful of regional sites. Public counts vary; insider estimate is fewer than 25 concentrate facilities worldwide [unverified specific count]
- **Bottling Investments Group (BIG)** historically held company-owned bottlers; the group has been wound down or refocused, with current BIG footprint limited to **South/Southeast Asia and parts of Africa** — not North America
- **Ingredient inbound and concentrate outbound logistics** — a real but compact yard footprint compared to a bottler network

### What The Bottlers Operate (Where The Yards Are)
- **Coca-Cola Consolidated (public, KO-adjacent):** **13 manufacturing facilities, 80 distribution and warehouse locations** across 14 states + DC; opened a $90M, 400,000-sq-ft Columbus OH DC in 2024 with on-site Vertique semi-automation
- **Coca-Cola UNITED:** ~52 facilities across six Southeastern states
- **Reyes Coca-Cola Bottling, Liberty, Great Lakes, Heartland, Swire (Western US):** each operates dozens of plants and DCs
- Across the North American system: **~70 independent bottlers, 50+ production facilities, 350+ distribution centers, 1.3B+ physical cases** (per TCCC's own 2017 refranchising disclosure)

### CEPG: The Mechanism That Makes Coe The Right Buyer
The **Cross Enterprise Procurement Group** is the joint procurement body where TCCC and the bottlers buy together — packaging, fleet, all modes of transportation and logistics, IT, MRO, fuels. Coe is **Vice Chairman**. CEPG-negotiated contracts cover **86–90% of system unit case sales** and **$19B+ in spend across 33 initiatives**. A YardFlow contract negotiated through CEPG is the rare path that gets a single piece of yard software adopted **across the bottler federation** without 70 individual sales cycles.

### Adjacent IT Layer: CONA Services
**CONA (Coke One North America)** is the bottler-owned shared IT services org running a unified SAP S/4HANA platform (cutover April 2024) for **11 North American bottlers** including Coca-Cola Consolidated and Coca-Cola UNITED. CONA is the technical surface where any system-wide YardFlow integration would land — it is the WMS/TMS backbone YardFlow would feed into. Coe doesn't run CONA, but CONA-compatible integrations make adoption frictionless across the participating bottlers.

## 3. Current State, Initiatives, & Pain Points

### The Microsoft AI Deal ($1.1B, April 2024)
- Five-year, **$1.1B agreement with Microsoft** — 4.4× the prior 2020 deal — covering Azure OpenAI Service across supply chain optimization, manufacturing, and marketing
- John Murphy (President & CFO) framed it as "ongoing digital transformation"
- This is a **TCCC-level deal**, but the supply chain use cases (demand forecasting, inventory optimization, distribution logistics) only deliver if the **bottlers** consume them. Yard data is one of the few feeds Microsoft Azure does not have a clean answer for at the facility level — it's an obvious gap to surface

### The Customer Demand & Supply Planning (CDSP) Rollout
- TCCC's CDSP system is **live in Spain and Germany** with ML forecasting and scenario planning — being expanded
- Pattern matches General Mills + Palantir digital twin: macro visibility is being built; the **last mile of facility-level yard data** is the visible gap

### Stated 2025 Priorities (Coe's own list)
1. Team
2. Sustainability
3. **Digitisation and digitalisation**
4. Productivity
5. Stakeholder support
6. Top-level supplier relationships
7. **Continuity of supply**

YardFlow maps cleanly to **#3 (digitisation), #4 (productivity), and #7 (continuity)** — and a quieter case for #2 (Scope 3 idle emissions).

### Q1 2026 Earnings — The Cost-Pressure Backdrop
- TCCC reported **comparable gross margin down ~30 bps** on commodity pressure (tea, coffee) and inventory timing
- Bottler partners absorbing aluminum and PET cost pressure
- Earnings call referenced a **"disruption playbook"** centered on **cross-enterprise procurement and productivity initiatives** — verbatim CEPG language. Coe's house
- Comparable operating profit grew **~12%**, margin expanded **~70 bps to 33.8%** despite the headwinds — productivity is being delivered, but the bar keeps rising

### Sustainability Pressure
- 2024 Scope 3 emissions: **~11.1B kg CO2e**, with **~8.3B kg from franchises** (i.e., the bottlers and their logistics)
- TCCC has committed to a **25% absolute Scope 1+2+3 reduction by 2030** vs. 2015 baseline (SBTi-aligned)
- EV100 member: transitioning own car/van fleet to EV by 2030
- The Scope 3 math is unavoidable: **bottler logistics dominates the inventory** [unverified breakdown beyond "franchises" disclosure]. Anything that cuts truck idle and dwell at bottler yards is directly Scope 3 material

### What's Missing From The Public Record
- **No public yard management system disclosure for TCCC concentrate plants** — small footprint, may already be solved
- **One known bottler YMS deployment in the global system: INFORM SYNCROSUPPLY at Coca-Cola HBC Austria's Edelstal plant**, with **45% gate-to-gate throughput reduction** and rollout to Romania, Czech Republic, Italy, Serbia. **This is European, not North American, and it's HBC, not a US bottler.** It's a useful proof point but not competitive lock-in in the US
- **No public North American bottler-wide YMS standard** — this is the open territory

### Pain Points (Inferred)
1. **Bottler heterogeneity.** 70 bottlers, varying scale, varying tech maturity. CONA standardizes ERP for 11 of them; yard ops are not on a common platform
2. **Volume concentration at remaining bottlers.** Refranchising consolidated US bottling into fewer, larger operators (Consolidated alone runs 80 DCs). Larger bottlers = more trailers per yard = more yard pressure
3. **Carrier-experience pressure.** Bottlers run mixed private/3PL fleets. In tight markets, drivers will deprioritize sites with long dwell — same dynamic as General Mills, magnified by DSD frequency
4. **The CDSP / Azure visibility gap.** Macro forecasting and inventory optimization assume reliable execution at the dock. Yard delays break the assumption set
5. **Sustainability accounting gap.** Scope 3 attribution to logistics inside franchise networks is still weak; bottlers cannot easily tell TCCC how many idle-hours they are emitting

## 4. The YardFlow Opportunity — Where The Pitch Actually Lands

### The Honest Framing
Coe is not the easiest sale in our pipeline. He doesn't directly operate the yards we'd standardize. **But he's the only person in the Coca-Cola system who can, in one decision, route a yard standard through CEPG to 200+ bottlers globally and 70 in North America.** Every other path requires winning bottlers one at a time.

This makes the YardFlow Coca-Cola pitch fundamentally **a system-standard pitch, not an operations pitch.** That is unusual for us, and it should change tone.

### Comparable That Lands Hardest Here
The 237-facility North American CPG anchor we displaced an incumbent at is the right comparable for Coca-Cola Consolidated and Coca-Cola UNITED specifically — **DSD beverage networks with mixed manufacturing + DC + cross-dock yard profiles**. The public Primo Brands comparable is also fair game given direct bottled-beverage adjacency.

### Pitch Architecture
1. **Pitch the standard, not the deployment.** YardFlow as a CEPG-blessed yard execution platform that any bottler can adopt with consistent data outputs back to CEPG/CONA
2. **Anchor to CONA.** A YardFlow integration into CONA's S/4HANA backbone = a single technical lift that lights up 11 bottlers. "We integrated to Manhattan and SAP at the 237-facility anchor; CONA is the same pattern"
3. **Connect to the Microsoft Azure deal.** YardFlow yard-execution data is the missing facility-level signal feeding CDSP and Azure OpenAI demand/inventory models. We're additive to the $1.1B Microsoft deal, not competitive
4. **Quantify Scope 3.** Bottler idle-hour reduction → reportable Scope 3 contribution to TCCC's 25%-by-2030 commitment. This is the angle Coe's sustainability priority #2 cares about
5. **Continuity of supply.** Frame yard execution as a **continuity layer** — when freight markets tighten, the bottlers with shorter driver wait times keep capacity. This is Coe's "all-weather" vocabulary

### What We Are Not Pitching
- Not pitching a TCCC-direct deployment at concentrate plants (footprint too small to be meaningful at his scale)
- Not pitching to displace CONA or SAP (we sit alongside)
- Not pitching as primary AI/ML platform (Microsoft owns that real estate; we are the data feed)
