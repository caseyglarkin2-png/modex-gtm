- **2 million sq ft** total across two yards
- **100+ dock doors** combined
- **550 trailer positions** combined
- **Mixed dry and refrigerated** trailer fleet — this is the specialty-retail tell. Cost Plus sells wine, gourmet food, and beverages alongside furniture and home decor; the refrigerated capability is what enables the food/wine assortment to flow through the same network as the dry goods.

This is a **2-DC network running mixed-temperature flow at 250-store retail throughput** — structurally identical to the small-DC-count, high-density specialty-retail footprint where yard execution is the dominant operating cost.

### Inbound Profile (What Hits Their Yards)
- **Heavy international inbound** — Cost Plus is fundamentally an importer. Furniture, textiles, ceramics from Southeast Asia and India; food and wine from Europe and South America; home decor from across the globe. Inbound is **ocean container → port → DC** as the dominant flow.
- **Wine, beer, and gourmet food** in the assortment means an FDA-regulated and TTB-regulated inbound stream alongside dry consumer goods. Yard scheduling has to keep refrigerated inbound separated and prioritized.
- **Bursty seasonality** — Q4 holiday (gifts, decor, wine), Q1 spring resets, mid-summer back-to-school adjacencies. Inbound trailer cadence at both DCs spikes hard for these windows.
- **Domestic outbound** — store-replenishment loads from each DC to ~125 stores apiece (roughly half-and-half east-west split).
- **No published private fleet of meaningful scale** — the case study explicitly notes that PINC enabled "private fleet trailer pool reduction." Cost Plus runs primarily on common carriers and contracted regional fleets.

### Tech Stack (What's Public, What's Inferred)
- **YMS (incumbent, confirmed):** **Kaleris (PINC)** Enterprise YMS with RFID gate reads, RFID-equipped Tracker appliances on yard tractors, and IoT integration. Deployed across both DCs. **This is the displacement target.**
- **Yard tractors:** RFID interrogator hardware mounted inside yard trucks used for trailer repositioning. Hardware footprint to consider in any rip-and-replace transition.
- **WMS / TMS:** [unverified — not disclosed in the Kaleris case study]. Specialty retail at this scale typically runs Manhattan, Blue Yonder, or HighJump-lineage systems; Cost Plus has not publicly disclosed.
- **Refrigerated unit management:** PINC's "refrigerated unit prioritization" is called out as a soft-saving — meaning their team currently uses the YMS to triage reefer trailers. Any successor system has to handle reefer prioritization day-one.
- **OMS / e-commerce fulfillment:** worldmarket.com operates as a meaningful DTC channel, fulfilled from the same DC network. [unverified — extent of dedicated e-commerce fulfillment infrastructure inside the DCs]

### The Kingswood-Era Operating Reality
Cost Plus has been a private-equity portfolio company since January 2021. Kingswood Capital is a middle-market operational-PE shop, not a financial-engineering shop — they buy operating businesses and run them. This matters for the YardFlow pitch in two ways:

1. **Capex discipline is real.** Any new system has to clear an IRR hurdle that PE-portfolio CFOs apply rigorously. Steve will need to walk in with a clear payback model, not a transformation narrative.

## 3. Current State, Initiatives, and Pain Points

### What's Likely on Steve's Plate Right Now
1. **Kingswood operating partner reviews** — quarterly P&L conversations where DC operating cost is a recurring line item. Yard-related costs (yard-driver labor, detention, fuel, fleet pool) are exactly the categories most-frequently questioned in these reviews.
2. **2-DC network running at 250-store retail throughput** with no obvious capex appetite from a PE owner to add a third DC. Same pattern as several other Wave-1 retail accounts: the network footprint is fixed; throughput-per-yard has to rise.
3. **Mixed-temperature inbound complexity** — the dry/refrigerated mix is operationally harder than pure-dry CPG yards, and refrigerated load spoilage is one of the few categories where a yard-execution miss creates a write-down rather than a soft inefficiency.
4. **Aging incumbent YMS** — the PINC deployment dates to the mid-2010s. The IoT/RFID hardware is reaching natural refresh cycles. Hardware refresh is the **natural opening** to re-evaluate the software platform.
5. **Kaleris consolidation overhang** — Kaleris is the rolled-up parent of PINC + ShipXpress + RailcarRx. PINC-original customers have been navigating product roadmap changes and platform consolidation since the rebranding. Customer-side fatigue with vendor M&A is a recurring theme worth probing.

### Macro Pressures on the Account
- **Specialty retail margin compression** is industry-wide. Cost Plus competes with Crate & Barrel, World Market's own DTC channel, HomeGoods/TJX, and Amazon for the same imported-decor share of wallet. DC operating cost is one of the few levers that doesn't trade off against merchandising.
- **Imported-goods tariff exposure** — Cost Plus is structurally exposed to any port disruption or tariff shift on Southeast Asian / European imports. Yard-execution agility (faster discharge, faster cross-dock to store-replen) becomes more valuable in volatile-import periods.
- **PE-owned portfolio company in year-5 of ownership** — Kingswood's typical hold period creates pressure to drive a credible operating-improvement narrative before any exit. A successful YMS displacement with documented hard savings reads well in a CIM.

### AI / Tech Stance (Inferred)
Steve's published quotes are pre-AI-era and pragmatic-operator in tone. He is not going to engage with a "generative AI yard" pitch. He will engage with:
- Specific dollar quantifications of yard-driver labor reduction
- Specific dwell-reduction numbers tied to detention savings
- Hardware-footprint comparisons (do we need to keep his existing tractor-mounted Trackers? what's the migration path?)
- Carrier-facing experience improvements (driver wait, gate transaction time, appointment compliance)

The pitch register that works is **engineer-to-engineer, dollars-to-dollars**, not visionary.

### Pain Points — Specific to This Account
1. **PINC platform is the rolled-up incumbent.** Steve has been on it for ~10+ years. Whatever architectural limits he has hit, he has hit them many times.
2. **2-DC network = compound effect.** Every minute of yard inefficiency at Stockton and Windsor compounds across the entire 550-trailer-position footprint.
3. **Refrigerated trailer prioritization is operationally critical** — Cost Plus sells wine, cheese, and gourmet food. A reefer load lost to yard mismanagement is a P&L hit, not just a soft cost. Steve's team handles this in PINC today but the workflow is whatever PINC enables, not whatever the workflow ideally is.
4. **Common-carrier-driven inbound** means driver experience at the gate directly affects carrier-availability pricing. A yard that turns trucks fast pays less for capacity.
5. **PE-owner capex discipline** means any rip-and-replace has to clear a higher hurdle than the original PINC purchase did.

### What Steve Is *Not* Going to Care About
- Generic "AI for yard" pitches without dollar quantification
- Transformation narratives that don't start with "here's what your existing PINC hardware becomes in our deployment"
- Anything that requires more change-management on his ops team than the math justifies
- Vendors who don't acknowledge the political reality that he bought PINC — going in pretending he didn't is a credibility-killer

### The Specific Angle for Steve

**Frame YardFlow as the natural successor to the system he himself bought — and as the credit-extending exit, not the criticism, of the PINC era.**

> "You stood up PINC ten years ago and cut yard-driver headcount, fleet pool, and fuel cost. The case study still ranks as one of the cleanest YMS ROI stories in the industry. The work you did is the *starting point* — the platform you built it on has been acquired twice, consolidated into a roll-up, and hasn't kept pace with what's possible today. ### Talking Points (Ranked)
1. **"We read your Kaleris case study. The hard-savings line items you delivered ten years ago are still the right framework. Let's talk about what the next round looks like on a modern platform."** Opens by acknowledging him, not selling him.

3. **"2 million sq ft, 100+ doors, 550 trailer positions, dry-and-reefer — we run the exact shape at a 237-facility CPG network. The reefer prioritization is the part most YMS vendors get wrong."** Pattern-match credibility.
4. **"What does your existing tractor-mounted RFID hardware become? Short answer: keep it. Long answer: here's how we migrate."** Pre-empts the hardware-investment objection.
5. **"For a PE-portfolio capex justification, here's the dollar model your CFO will recognize."** PE-speak.
6. **"Kingswood's operating partners are going to ask about this category at next review. You can either bring them a fresh hard-savings story or wait until they ask why yard cost hasn't moved."** Reframes inaction as a risk.

### Watch Out For
- **Don't lead with the displacement.** Lead with the case-study acknowledgment. The displacement is the *implication*, not the headline.
- **Don't go around Steve.** Going to Kingswood operating partners, the CFO, or Eric Hunter / Barry J. Feld (whichever is current CEO) without Steve's awareness will end this account immediately. He is the path.

- **PE capex discipline cuts both ways.** Kingswood's IRR hurdle is the friend of a clean ROI case and the enemy of a transformation narrative. Bring the dollar model on the first call.
