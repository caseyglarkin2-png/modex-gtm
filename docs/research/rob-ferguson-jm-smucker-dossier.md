## 4. Known Logistics Pain Points

### 1. Hostess Plant Consolidation = Yard Pressure at Survivors
With Indianapolis closing, Cloverhill divested, and Voortman gone, the **Columbus, GA** and **Emporia, KS** plants are absorbing more volume. Columbus is also being **expanded** ($120M, completing 2027). When you concentrate sweet-baked-snacks production into fewer plants, you get:
- More daily inbound (raw materials, packaging) and outbound (DSD-adjacent shipments)
- More dock conflicts between in-house production and the legacy Hostess distribution model
- **Live, multi-year situation** — Ferguson owns it.

### 2. Multi-Temperature Network Complexity
Smucker runs four distinct temperature regimes through overlapping facilities:
- **Ambient** (Folgers, Jif, Smucker's, Hostess)
- **Frozen** (Uncrustables — McCalla, Longmont, Scottsville)
- **Refrigerated/wet** (Meow Mix wet cat food, Soft 'N Chewy)
- **Bulk raw** (green coffee beans into New Orleans)

The Buffalo plant alone is ambient + refrigerated. Yard operations have to coordinate trailer types, dock door temperature zones, and turn priorities across all of these.

### 3. Coffee — Single-Region Concentration Risk
**Nearly all** Smucker coffee production runs through **New Orleans + Suffolk**. Folgers is the #1 at-home coffee brand in the U.S. The yard at New Orleans handles the largest bulk coffee volume in the world. **Any yard inefficiency at NOLA cascades across Folgers, Dunkin', and Café Bustelo simultaneously** — there is no production redundancy.

### 4. The DSD Shadow at Hostess
Hostess's pre-acquisition model was DSD (direct-store-delivery), with route trucks pulling from depots multiple times a week. Smucker has been migrating volume to a more conventional DC-and-truckload model, but the transition is not complete. This creates **mixed yard environments** at Hostess plants where small DSD trucks share docks with full TL freight — highly inefficient unless explicitly orchestrated.

### 5. Pet Food Service-Level Recovery
The $30M Q3 FY2025 disruption was specifically a pet food problem — out-of-stocks at retail. After supply was restored, Smucker had to push hard to **replenish retailer inventories**. That kind of recovery period stresses yards: surge outbound, longer dwell, more dock contention. Ferguson's prior GM role gives him personal scar tissue here.

### 6. New Plant Ramp at McCalla
McCalla opened October 2024. New plants typically run **15-25% below optimal yard/dock utilization in their first 18 months** — McCalla is right in that window now (May 2026). This is the highest-leverage moment to bake YMS into the standard operating model before the bad habits set.

### 7. Continuous Re-Org Drag
Smucker has changed supply chain leadership structure **three times in 18 months**. That is genuinely hard on operating teams. Ferguson needs **fast, demonstrable wins** — yard turn-time reduction is the kind of metric that shows up clearly on quarterly reviews.
