## 4. Known Logistics Pain Points

### 1. HMGMA Outbound Compound Pressure
HMGMA was designed for 500K vehicles/year. The plant uses 850+ robots and 300+ AGVs **inside** the building, but the **vehicle compound and rail-loading yard outside** the wall are conventional finished-vehicle yard operations. As production climbs from one model (IONIQ 5) to three (IONIQ 9, plus Kia in 2026), compound dwell time and rail-spot scheduling become the throughput-limiting step.

### 2. Korean Import Surge → Port Compound Congestion
With ~1M Korea-built vehicles still flowing through Brunswick, Wilmington, Newark, Baltimore, and Tacoma annually, **Glovis-operated VPCs have visible dwell-time problems**. Industry data shows:
- Vessel-arrival-to-gate-out averaging **~10.1 days** at major auto ports
- **2.4 days** waiting to berth, **6.5 days** dwell after discharge
- Sudden tariff-driven "pull-ahead" shipments (loading vehicles before potential tariff hikes) compound this

### 3. Rail-Yard Spot Scheduling
HMGMA ships majority by rail. Auto-rack rail spotting at the plant compound and at receiving VPCs is a **classic yard management gap**:
- Rail-cars arrive in waves; loading is throughput-constrained by rack availability and labor
- Empty-car turn at the plant directly affects how many vehicles HMGMA can ship per day
- CSX, NS, and G&W all want predictable spot-and-pull schedules; Glovis's compound visibility into when the next rail-rack is loadable is **operationally critical, technologically uneven**

### 4. Multi-Brand, Multi-Compound Coordination
HMGMA will produce Hyundai, Kia, and Genesis. Glovis must segregate by brand, by destination region, by dealer allocation, by carrier (rail vs. truck), and by trim. This is **a yard segmentation problem at scale** — and Carey's allocation system is the upstream input.

### 5. Tariff-Driven Network Flux
If the US-Korea tariff settles back to 25%, more production shifts to HMGMA and HMMA, *but* import volume doesn't drop instantly — it lingers in the pipeline. Carey's network will run **hot on both ends** for 12–24 months: existing Korea-built inventory needs to clear domestic compounds while new HMGMA volume floods the same downstream rail and dealer network.

### 6. Final-Mile Truck Delivery to Dealers
HMA has ~835 dealers nationwide. Final-mile car-haul (auto-transport trucks) picks up from VPCs and rail-ramps. **Dealer delivery accuracy** is one of Carey's KPIs (he owns the allocation system) — but dwell time at the rail-ramp-to-truck handoff directly degrades on-time-to-dealer.

## 5. Yard Management & Compound Operations Intelligence

### What We Know
- Glovis America has **pilot interest in advanced logistics tech** (XCIENT hydrogen trucks, AI-optimized HMGMA inbound) — but **no public announcement of a yard management system at the VPC/compound level**
- Industry data: tracking systems at high-volume vehicle export hubs reduce retrieval times **>40%** and improve gate-to-vessel cycle — Glovis has likely done some of this internally, but it is not publicly visible
- The **gate-in / yard-storage / rail-load / truck-load / gate-out** sequence at a VPC is functionally identical to a CPG yard problem — only the SKU is a 4,000-pound vehicle instead of a trailer
- **The Savannah and LA Glovis hubs that opened in April 2026 are brand-new operations** — yard SOPs are still being written. Greenfield = ideal time to install YardFlow

### The Visibility Gap
HMA/Glovis has best-in-class capability in:
- Vessel-tracking and ocean carrier visibility
- Rail TMS (rail-side has its own AEI tracking)
- Dealer DMS integration for allocation
- ❌ **VPC compound visibility** — where each VIN sits, how long it has been there, what is gating its release to the next leg

This is **structurally the same gap** that exists in every CPG yard — and Carey is uniquely positioned to feel it because his allocation accuracy depends on knowing what is actually movable from the compound on any given day.
