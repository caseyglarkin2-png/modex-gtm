## 4. Known Logistics Pain Points

### 1. Loaded-Reefer Hours: The Win Is Site-Level, Not Network-Level
The Kaleris 134,000-hour result was earned at sites — each Bob Evans plant yard got instrumented and trimmed. **What that result is not:** a network-wide operating model. The same dwell-reduction discipline is not yet running at the same intensity at Michael Foods plants, or Crystal Farms plants, or Potato Products of Idaho, or 8th Avenue. The asymmetry across the Post Holdings cold-chain footprint is the gap, and it is exactly the gap that creates the multi-OpCo expansion case.

### 2. The Acquisition-Absorption Yard Problem
Every Post acquisition since 2018 has brought a different yard operating model into the family. **8th Avenue** (closed July 2025) has its own pasta and peanut-butter plants, its own carriers, its own yard practices. **Potato Products of Idaho** (closed March 2025) is a refrigerated-potato production node now flowing into the same Refrigerated Retail demand picture as Bob Evans' own potato lines. Neither of these acquisitions was standardized onto a parent yard stack at close, because there isn't one. **The cost of running multiple yard operating models inside one Refrigerated Retail segment compounds quietly until somebody adds it up.** The new COO will eventually add it up.

### 3. Refrigerated Trailer Genset Burn = Real Scope 1 Number
Every hour a loaded reefer sits idling with the genset on is fuel burning to maintain temp. Kaleris' published case study attributed ~$471,000 in fuel and M&R savings to the dwell reduction — that is a real number, and it is also a real **GHG number**. Post Holdings has not been as publicly aggressive on emissions disclosure as a Nestlé or PepsiCo, but the SEC climate-disclosure regime and customer-side pressure (Walmart Project Gigaton, Kroger, Albertsons) make this a number worth knowing about and improving. **Network-wide reefer-dwell reduction is a network-wide Scope 1 reduction.**

### 4. USDA Traceability Demand vs. Yard Visibility
USDA-inspected pork facilities are under continuing pressure to shorten recall response time. The single point at which lot-level traceability most often breaks is **the trailer in the yard between dock and gate** — once it leaves the dock seal, paper-based or scattered-system tracking is where errors happen. **Yard visibility = recall-readiness.** This is the framing that gets Quality and Food Safety on the team, not just Transportation.

### 5. Multi-Temp Dock Contention
Bob Evans plants ship a mix of chilled side dishes and frozen breakfast SKUs. Different trailers, different dock door requirements, different temp-zone integrity. **The yard is where temp-zone integrity is preserved or lost.** A trailer mis-staged is a trailer mis-staged whether it costs you a dock turn or a temp deviation; the latter is a quality event. Yard execution sits upstream of both.

### 6. Carrier Mix Heterogeneity
Bob Evans uses a mix of dedicated, asset-based common carriers, and brokered loads. Each carrier brings its own driver app, telematics, and check-in expectations. **Standardized gate-in → dock-in across that carrier mix is a YardFlow-shaped problem.** Kaleris built the system that runs the yard at each site; YardFlow's question is whether the *operating model on top of* that system is consistent across carriers and consistent across plants.

### 7. The Operating-Model Gap Above the YMS
This is the most important one and the cleanest YardFlow wedge. **A YMS instruments a yard. It does not standardize an operating model across yards.** The Kaleris case study is a single-customer, multi-site Kaleris deployment — it is not a network-wide yard operating model with KPIs that roll up to Refrigerated Retail segment leadership. That layer is unbuilt at Post Holdings today. It is the layer YardFlow exists to be.
