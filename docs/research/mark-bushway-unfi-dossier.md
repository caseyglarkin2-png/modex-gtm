### Legacy-UNFI vs. Legacy-SuperValu — Why the Network Is Uneven
Pre-2018, UNFI was the natural/organic specialist — east coast and west coast DC footprint serving Whole Foods, independent natural grocers, and a smaller conventional book. Its DC fingerprint reflected that profile: smaller average facility size, refrigerated-heavy, geographically clustered near the natural-food customer base.

SuperValu was the opposite — large conventional-grocery wholesale DCs across the Midwest and Plains, serving Cub Foods, Shoppers, Hornbacher's, Hy-Vee historically, and a long tail of independent conventional grocers. The SuperValu DCs were the inheritance that took UNFI from ~30 DCs to ~60 overnight and brought with them the **PINC RFID / Kaleris-generation yard tooling** documented in the publicly-available PINC customer list and Kaleris/Daimler case studies. That generation of yard tech solved 2010-era site-by-site problems — RFID tag visibility on trailers inside one fenceline — and was never designed for the network-level operating model UNFI's combined footprint now requires.

The integration is still active. UNFI is closing legacy SuperValu Plains DCs (Billings, Bismarck), legacy UNFI east-coast facilities (Logan Township → Allentown → next consolidation), and Midwest distribution (Fort Wayne, Sturtevant). Each closure is a **forced yard-protocol decision** at the receiving facility, because the inbound trailer pool changes shape with every closure.

### Distribution Categories Served
- **Natural / organic specialty:** the original UNFI book — Whole Foods, Natural Grocers, Sprouts (historical), independent co-ops, specialty regional chains
- **Conventional grocery wholesale:** the SuperValu inheritance — Cub Foods (UNFI-owned), Shoppers (UNFI-owned), regional conventional chains, independent grocers across the Midwest and Plains
- **Foodservice & specialty channels:** e-commerce, military, c-store, foodservice

- **Mixed private fleet + carrier mix** — UNFI runs its own fleet for outbound to retail and supplements with contract carriers
- **Heavy inbound complexity:** UNFI receives from manufacturers across natural/organic specialty (small-volume, high-SKU-count, brittle supply chains) and conventional CPG (high-volume, scheduled, FTL-dominant) at the same dock doors at many facilities
- **Retail-banner outbound:** Cub Foods and Shoppers are UNFI-owned banners — their store-replenishment operates on different cadences than the wholesale-customer accounts, which adds a second outbound traffic pattern at the DCs that serve them

## 4. Known Logistics Pain Points

### 1. The Two-Generation Yard Tech Problem
The SuperValu DCs that joined UNFI in 2018 came with **PINC / Kaleris-era yard tooling** designed for 2010-era trailer-tracking. The legacy-UNFI DCs that predated the acquisition have a different stack again — partly because the natural/organic side of the business never had the same RFID build-out investment that conventional grocery wholesale received. Then a **third generation** of facilities (Manchester PA, Sarasota FL, the new builds) is being commissioned with whatever the current standard is. The result: **three generations of yard tooling running across the same enterprise**, on the same closure-and-consolidation timeline that is shoving more trailers per dock through the surviving facilities.

This is the cleanest "modernization wedge" in the YardFlow target list. The PINC/Kaleris generation is not bad technology — it was the right answer for what was being asked at that point — but it was never designed for the network-level operating model UNFI now needs.

### 2. Closure-Driven Throughput Compression
The closure pattern is **inherently a yard-density problem**:
- Logan Township (2023) → volume into Allentown
- Allentown (2026) → volume into the next regional consolidation point
- Billings + Bismarck (2024) → volume into Plains regional facilities still operating
- Fort Wayne (Feb 2025) → volume redistributed across the Central region
- Sturtevant (Aug 2026) → volume into surviving Midwest DCs

Each closure means more trailers per door per day at the receiving facility. UNFI is shrinking its physical footprint while serving a customer base that **did not get smaller** (sales outlook raised post-cyberattack). The throughput-per-surviving-dock number is the metric that compounds across every consolidation decision.

### 3. The Cyberattack Resilience Test
Every operational technology proposal at UNFI in 2026 has to answer: what is the manual fallback when systems go dark? A yard management system that requires constant cloud connectivity for gate decisions is harder to land in this room than one that has a local-cache mode or a documented degraded-operating procedure. The June 2024 event is not in the rearview mirror; it is the **screening criterion** for vendor evaluation.

### 4. The Mixed-Banner Outbound Pattern
UNFI's outbound traffic is fundamentally not one pattern — it is at least four:
- Wholesale to Whole Foods (high-volume, scheduled, multi-temp, predictable cadence)
- Wholesale to independent natural grocers (lower-volume, more sites per route, brittle SKU mix)
- Wholesale to conventional grocers (legacy SuperValu customer base — higher-volume, scheduled)
- Replenishment to UNFI's own retail banners (Cub Foods, Shoppers — different cadence again)

The yards at facilities serving multiple of these patterns simultaneously have to stage trailers for fundamentally different outbound rhythms. Generic yard tooling that assumes "one site, one outbound pattern" misses how the work actually flows.

### 5. The Manufacturer Inbound Mix
On the inbound side, UNFI is the recipient of two completely different supplier ecosystems sharing the same dock doors:
- **Natural/organic specialty manufacturers** — small-to-mid-sized CPG brands, often single-source, often arriving in LTL or less-than-FTL configurations, with brittle backup capacity
- **Conventional grocery CPG** — Kraft Heinz, General Mills, Tyson, etc., arriving in FTL on scheduled lanes with predictable cadences

These two inbound patterns compete for the same receiving slots. A yard that doesn't differentiate them ends up either over-prioritizing the FTL conventional inbound (and starving the specialty inbound that has worse downstream resilience) or letting the specialty inbound monopolize dock minutes (and burning detention dollars on the FTL conventional reefers waiting outside).

### 6. The David Best Retail Banner Spin-Up
With a dedicated President & CEO of Retail running Cub Foods / Shoppers (Aug 2025 onward), the retail side of UNFI is being operationally separated from the wholesale side. But the **DCs are shared**. The yard does not know whether the trailer leaving is going to a Whole Foods, an independent natural grocer, or a Cub Foods store — but the routing-prioritization decision has been pushed up to two different Presidents' offices now. Yard tooling that surfaces the right ownership lens at the right moment becomes a tool for inter-divisional coordination, not just an operations tool.

### 7. Cold Chain Compliance Through the Yard
The previous outreach to Mark (March 28, 2026) named the cold-chain gap at the gate-to-dock layer. The framing was on-target: trailers carrying refrigerated and frozen product can sit in a yard for hours before anyone confirms temp at the chassis. For a company that distributes to Whole Foods (where cold chain compliance is brand-defining) and to the Cub Foods / Shoppers banner stores (where it is the standard food-safety obligation), yard-time-as-cold-chain-time is **the same problem stated two ways**. Throughput optimization and cold-chain compliance are the same conversation.
