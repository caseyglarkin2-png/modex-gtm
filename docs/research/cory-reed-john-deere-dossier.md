## 4. Known Logistics Pain Points

### 1. Greenfield Yard Pressure at Hebron
The Hebron, IN facility is **1.2M sq ft on 234 acres** — that's roughly half its total acreage in pavement, dock apron, and trailer parking. New parts DCs typically run **15–25% below optimal dock utilization for the first 12–18 months** as carrier patterns, dock scheduling, and yard ops stabilize. Hebron opens within Reed's first 12 months in role. Standing up a YMS as part of the SOP at day one is materially cheaper than retrofitting.

### 2. Milan: 450,000 Orders/Week Through One Site
Milan ships **up to 450,000 orders weekly** across 800,000+ SKUs. That order volume implies aggressive inbound/outbound trailer flow — easily 200–400 trailer movements per day. The Milan campus has been operating since 1973; yard layout reflects 50+ years of accreted decisions, not a clean-sheet design. Even modest dwell-time improvements compound at this scale.

### 3. Oversize Outbound from Factory Yards
Waterloo, East Moline, Davenport, Dubuque, Augusta — these factory yards stage **finished tractors, combines, excavators, and dump trucks** for outbound to dealers. Oversize freight has unique yard requirements: trailer length, turning radius, escorted moves, permit timing. A trailer carrying a 9R tractor cannot be shuffled like a dry-van of cereal. Yard scheduling for oversize is a specialized problem and historically managed via clipboards and radios at most heavy-equipment OEMs.

### 4. Tariff-Driven Inbound Volatility
$600M of tariff exposure means inbound parts flow is constantly being rerouted, mode-switched, and re-sourced. Yards become buffers when supply chains thrash. **A yard with no real-time visibility is a yard accumulating cost during disruption** — and Deere is in disruption.

### 5. Right-to-Repair Compliance Deadline (Dec 31, 2026)
The settlement requires expanded diagnostic and parts access for non-dealer repair channels by year-end 2026. This will likely **increase parts-order volume and complexity** through Milan and Hebron — more SKUs going to more endpoints (independent shops, end-customers) instead of consolidated dealer-bulk shipments. More small-parcel-friendly outbound = more dock / yard complexity.

### 6. Dealer Inventory Whiplash
"Producing to demand" was Deere's Q4 2024 mantra. Production cuts at Waterloo, then partial recall in March 2026, signal a stop-start operating environment. Yard operations at factories are downstream of these production changes — when the line restarts, finished-goods staging surges.

### 7. Kernersville Greenfield (Same Story as Hebron)
The new excavator factory in NC is a clean-sheet site. Outbound finished-excavator yard staging, inbound parts from new supplier base, a freshly-trained workforce — this is the easiest place in the company to deploy a yard system at zero retrofit cost.

## 5. Yard Management & Dock Scheduling Intelligence

### What We Know
- **No public mention** of a deployed yard management system across Deere's manufacturing or distribution footprint
- Deere runs a sophisticated **SAP-based parts inventory and order-management system** at Milan and across regional depots — strong inside-the-walls visibility
- Deere participates in **DOT FLOW** for container/freight visibility — strong in-transit visibility
- The **gap between TMS in-transit data and SAP/WMS in-warehouse data** — the yard — appears unmanaged at the platform level [unverified — based on absence of public disclosure rather than confirmed absence]

### The Visibility Gap Specific to Heavy-Equipment OEMs
Unlike CPG, Deere's outbound yard problem includes:
- **Oversize trailers** that cannot be staged in standard dock layouts
- **Permit-coordinated dispatch** (some loads can only move during specific hours/days based on state DOT permits)
- **Dealer-direct deliveries** rather than retail DC consolidation — so trailer-to-dealer-route matching matters at the yard
- **Mixed inbound:** raw materials (steel coil), supplier parts (precision sensors from India), reman cores (returned from dealers)

These factors make a heavy-equipment yard meaningfully more complex than a CPG yard — and arguably a higher-value target for systematic yard management.

## 6. Key Insight: What Makes Cory Reed Care About Yard Management?

### Primary Hook: The Title Itself
"Lifecycle Solutions, Supply Management, and Customer Success" is a single P&L combining aftermarket, supply chain, and customer experience. The yard is **literally where parts trucks idle on the way to keeping a customer's combine running during harvest**. There is no other executive at Deere whose mandate maps more cleanly to yard-throughput economics.

### The Two Greenfield Projects (Hebron + Kernersville)
Both major facility announcements landed in the first 100 days of Reed's tenure. **Both open in 2026.** Standing up a yard management system as part of the day-one SOP is a once-in-a-decade opportunity. Pitching YMS to the executive who personally signs off on greenfield commissioning is the highest-leverage timing possible.

### The $600M Tariff Pressure → Productivity Mandate
With $600M of tariff drag and a 12% revenue decline, every productivity lever inside supply chain has to be pulled. **Reed cannot get more revenue this year — he can only get more efficient.** Yard productivity is one of the few unfought battlegrounds.

### Right-to-Repair Compliance Increases Yard Complexity
By Dec 31, 2026, Deere must ship parts and diagnostics access to a broader set of endpoints. **More fragmented outbound flow = more yard complexity.** A yard system that handles small-parcel outbound alongside dealer-bulk shipments is exactly what the new compliance environment requires.

### The Connected-Machines Strategic Frame
Reed's "precision upgrade" mandate is about converting the 600,000-machine connected fleet into recurring revenue. Every minute a parts truck idles in the yard is a minute that connected-machine downtime extends at the customer site. **YardFlow is the supply-chain leg of the connected-machines value proposition.**

### The Dealer-Carrier Relationship Risk
Deere's dealers rely on parts arriving from Milan in tight windows during harvest and planting. Carriers who experience long waits will deprioritize Deere loads. In a constrained-demand environment, **carrier capacity and dealer goodwill are both finite** — yard efficiency protects both simultaneously.

### The Margin Math
Conservative estimate: Milan alone moves ~250 trailers per day (450K orders/week ÷ ~7 working days ÷ ~250 orders per trailer). Cutting 24 minutes per turn × 250 trailers × 250 working days = **25,000 trailer-hours per year at one DC.** Apply across Milan + Hebron + ~10 major factory yards and the total is substantial — and translates directly into demurrage avoidance, carrier-rate protection, and Scope 3 emissions reduction.

### What He Will Not Be Sold By
- **Pure cost-out pitches** without a customer outcome — his title has "Customer Success" in it
- **Tech-for-tech-sake** — he built ISG, he knows the difference between platform investment and feature noise
- **Anti-dealer framing** — the dealer network is the bedrock of Deere's customer relationship; right-to-repair friction notwithstanding, no Deere executive will sit through a pitch that paints dealers as the problem
