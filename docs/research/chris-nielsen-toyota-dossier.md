### Parts Distribution & Logistics Network
- **~30+ parts distribution centers (PDCs)** across North America handling service parts
- **500+ Tier 1 suppliers** in North America — Toyota's supplier network is massive and deeply integrated
- **Milkrun logistics system** — Toyota pioneered the "milkrun" (循環集荷) model in the US where a single truck makes rounds to multiple suppliers picking up parts in sequence, rather than each supplier shipping independently
- **Cross-dock operations** at every assembly plant — inbound parts are received, sorted, and sequenced for line-side delivery
- **~$30 billion+ annual procurement** spend in North America (Nielsen managed this as VP of Purchasing)

### The Toyota Production System (TPS) Connection
TPS is the **DNA of everything Nielsen oversees**. Key principles directly relevant to yard management:

- **Just-in-Time (JIT)**: Parts arrive at the exact moment needed — measured in hours, not days. Plant-side yards are the **staging buffer** that makes JIT possible
- **Heijunka (平準化)**: Production leveling — requires smoothed, predictable inbound material flow, which means trailers must arrive and be unloaded on precise schedules
- **Jidoka (自働化)**: Stop-and-fix quality — if a yard bottleneck causes a line stoppage, it cascades through the entire production schedule. One late trailer can halt a $50M/day assembly line
- **Kanban**: Pull-based signaling — Toyota's kanban system extends to the yard, with electronic kanban triggering trailer movements from staging areas to dock doors
- **Muda (无駄)**: Waste elimination — driver wait time, idle trailers, and congested yards are textbook muda. Nielsen would recognize this immediately as a TPS problem

## 4. Known Logistics Pain Points & YardFlow Relevance

### Pain Point 1: JIT Precision at Scale — The Yard Is the Execution Layer
- Toyota runs the **tightest JIT system in the world** — parts inventory at plant is measured in **2-4 hours** of production, not days
- This means the **yard is not a parking lot — it's a precision buffer**. Every trailer in the yard has a specific dock appointment window, and every minute of delay compounds
- At TMMK Georgetown alone (~2,000 vehicles/day), a single production line runs at ~60 vehicles/hour. A 30-minute dock delay = 30 vehicles not built = **~$1M in delayed revenue**
- **YardFlow pitch**: "Your yard is the last mile of JIT. We make it as precise as the assembly line."

### Pain Point 2: Milkrun Coordination & Supplier Inbound Staging
- Toyota's milkrun system sends trucks on **fixed routes picking up from 5-10 suppliers per run** — these trucks arrive at the plant on tight schedules
- If a milkrun truck is delayed at the yard gate or can't find a dock, it creates a **cascading delay** across all subsequent supplier pickups
- **Cross-dock staging areas** at Toyota plants sort inbound parts from milkrun trucks into line-side delivery sequences — these areas are capacity-constrained
- **YardFlow pitch**: "Every minute your milkrun truck waits at the gate is a minute that cascades across your entire supplier network."

### Pain Point 3: New Facility Buildout (TBMNC Battery Plant)
- The $13.9B Liberty, NC battery plant is a **greenfield opportunity** — yard management design decisions are being made right now
- Battery cell production has unique logistics: raw lithium/nickel/cobalt materials inbound, finished battery modules outbound to assembly plants
- Hazmat considerations for battery materials add complexity to yard movements
- **YardFlow pitch**: "You're designing the supply chain for the future of Toyota. Let's make sure the yard is as advanced as the battery technology."

### Pain Point 4: Multi-Model / Multi-Powertrain Complexity
- Toyota's "multi-pathway" electrification strategy means plants now build ICE, hybrid, PHEV, and BEV variants on the **same production line**
- Each powertrain variant requires **different components from different suppliers** arriving in different sequences
- Georgetown alone now builds Camry (8th gen ICE sunset), Camry Hybrid (9th gen, all-hybrid from May 2024), RAV4 Hybrid, and Lexus ES
- More SKUs = more trailers = more complex yard choreography
- **YardFlow pitch**: "Multi-pathway means multi-complexity in your yard. We help you manage it without adding headcount."

### Pain Point 5: Finished Vehicle Logistics (FVL)
- Toyota produces ~1.8M vehicles/year in NA — every one needs to be staged, loaded onto auto haulers, and shipped to ~1,200+ dealers
- Finished vehicle yards at assembly plants are **separate but adjacent** to inbound logistics yards — coordination between the two is critical
- Auto hauler drivers face the same wait time problems as inbound carriers
- **YardFlow pitch**: "You cut build time on the line. We cut wait time at the dock. Together, that's a faster vehicle-to-dealer pipeline."

### Pain Point 6: Mazda-Toyota Shared Yard (Huntsville)

- Coordinating Mazda and Toyota inbound logistics, with different supplier networks and different production schedules, on one yard is a unique operational challenge

## 5. Yard Management & Dock Scheduling — Toyota Context

### What Toyota Currently Uses
- Toyota is famously **build-not-buy** for operational systems — TPS culture favors in-house solutions
- Toyota's logistics arm (Toyota Logistics Services) manages much of the carrier coordination
- Traditional **kanban-based yard control** — physical or electronic cards signal trailer movements
- Toyota likely uses a mix of **homegrown systems + SAP EWM** for warehouse/yard management at newer facilities
- Gate check-in processes at Toyota plants are known to be **manual or semi-automated** — badge + paper-based at many facilities

### The Build vs. Buy Tension
- Toyota's instinct is always to build internally (this is part of TPS — "develop your own people and systems")
- However, the **speed of expansion** (Liberty NC, Princeton retool, Huntsville JV) may outpace internal development capacity
- Nielsen's Wharton education and his "One Toyota" reorganization experience suggest he's pragmatic about when to build vs. buy
- **Key framing: YardFlow doesn't replace TPS — it digitizes it.** Frame YardFlow as a tool that **enables TPS principles** in the yard, not as something that replaces Toyota's way of working

### Driver Wait Time Reality
- Industry average truck turn time at manufacturing plants: **~90-120 minutes**
- Toyota likely performs better than average (~45-60 minutes) due to TPS discipline
- But even at Toyota, **the yard is the least digitized part of the supply chain** — assembly lines have real-time andon boards, but yards often still use clipboard check-ins
- YardFlow's value: cutting from 48 to 24 minutes is compelling even for a best-in-class operator, because at Toyota's scale, minutes = millions
