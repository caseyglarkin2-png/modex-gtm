## 4. The 3PL Adjustment — Why CJ Logistics Is a Different Kind of Target

### YardFlow's 3PL Thesis (vs. End-Shipper Pitch)
For end-shippers like Kraft Heinz or General Mills, YardFlow is a tool they buy and operate at their own facilities. For a 3PL like CJ Logistics America, YardFlow is something fundamentally different:

> **A capability they buy once and resell as a managed-service offering to their North American customer base.**

CJ Logistics' business model already does this with WMS (Blue Yonder, Korber, SAP EWM), OneTrack, AMRs, and the SVT integration platform. The TES function's published mandate is exactly this — embed third-party tech, wrap it in operational service, productize it for customers. **YardFlow fits this exact distribution model** — at the layer of the warehouse that today is mostly clipboards, radios, and disconnected dock-scheduling tools.

### Why This Is a Partnership Conversation, Not a Vendor Sale
A standard YMS pitch says: *"Replace your spreadsheet with our software."* That's a procurement conversation, and at CJ Logistics that conversation goes to PMO and gets sized for a single customer engagement.

The CJ Logistics pitch needs to say: *"Embed our yard-execution layer into your TES product catalog, the same way OneTrack sits inside it today. We bring a software stack and pricing model designed for multi-tenant 3PL deployment. You bring 70+ facilities, a Korean parent's $457M US capex program, and the customer relationships. We split the value."*

This reframes Adams' evaluation:
- Not: *"Do I want to buy this?"* (procurement instinct: defer to a customer-funded pilot)
- Instead: *"Is this the next layer in TES, the way OneTrack was the last one?"* (transformation instinct: lean in)

### Adams Specifically Is the Right Door
Other CJ Logistics America contacts route this elsewhere:
- **Brad Nuffer (COO)** — operational, but his Ops/PMO/Brokerage scope means a YardFlow pitch routes to procurement for a single-site test, not a strategic embed
- **Kevin Coleman (CEO)** — too senior for a cold opener; will delegate down to TES
- **Joe Smiesko (VP Safety)** — owns the safety case for OneTrack; not the right entry point for a yard-execution platform
- **Sam Sharp (EVP Customer Development)** — customer-facing, but pitches arrive at him **through** TES vetting, not around it

**Adams' entire function — TES + Continuous Improvement — is partnership-vetting and customer-lifecycle transformation.** Her published methodology, expressed through the OneTrack story:
1. Identify a customer-side pain point (safety + productivity at the four walls)
2. Pilot a partner technology at one site
3. Validate KPIs (73% safety event reduction, 11% UPH lift)
4. Scale across the network (40+ sites)
5. Use the scaled deployment as a customer-acquisition story

**This is exactly the path YardFlow needs to walk.** OneTrack is the most recent precedent, and it is fresh — the Feb 2025 announcement explicitly framed it as an ongoing expansion, not a closed chapter.

## 7. Why CJ Logistics America Matters Strategically to YardFlow

Most YardFlow targets are end-shippers — one logo, one yard footprint, one buying decision. CJ Logistics America is structurally different:

- **70+ North American facilities** under one decision-making roof
- **Multi-vertical customer base** (food & bev, healthcare, automotive, tire, CPG, retail, e-commerce) that CJ Logistics can in turn introduce to YardFlow
- **A standing TES function** with a published methodology that YardFlow's pilot model maps onto cleanly — and a fresh 40-site precedent (OneTrack) showing the funnel works
- **A leader (Adams) whose entire job is to find products that fit this exact pattern** — and who just had her scope expanded to include Continuous Improvement
- **A Korean parent capex program ($457M, NEXUS Project)** building new facilities to be TES-engineered from day one — meaning yard execution at those sites is uncommitted greenfield
- **No incumbent enterprise YMS** — meaningfully cleaner runway than Kenco, DHL, or other competing 3PL targets

If CJ Logistics America productizes YardFlow into TES the way OneTrack was productized, the unit economics of the partnership likely exceed any single end-shipper deal in YardFlow's pipeline. This is a 1-to-many distribution play disguised as a 1-to-1 sales conversation — with the additional advantage that, unlike Kenco, there is no entrenched competitive YMS to dislodge.

The question is not whether CJ Logistics needs YardFlow internally — they have functioning operations at the yard today and could keep running them. The question is whether **CJ Logistics' customers** would buy a CJ-branded yard-execution managed service alongside OneTrack, Blue Yonder/Korber WMS, and SVT-orchestrated automation — and whether YardFlow is the right partner to build it with.

That's the conversation to have with Laura Adams.
