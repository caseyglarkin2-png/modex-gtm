### Why the Yard Pattern Is Unusual at a Foodservice Distributor
PFG does not look like a CPG manufacturer's yard. It looks like the **opposite half of the supply chain**:
- **Inbound side (DC arrival):** Manufacturer trailers (General Mills, Kraft Heinz, Tyson, etc.) — high volume, multi-temp, full truckloads, scheduled arrivals
- **Outbound side (DC departure):** PFG's own fleet — **multi-stop, multi-temp routed delivery trucks** dropping at restaurants, schools, c-stores. Each truck might hit 8–15 stops on a route, mixing frozen/refrigerated/dry compartments
- **Yard congestion** therefore happens at **both ends of the day**: morning outbound staging (driver bay-out chaos at 3–5am) and afternoon inbound receiving (manufacturer drops fighting for door space against return trailers)
- Each DC also stages **bobtails, sleeper cabs, and reefer-running empties** that idle while waiting for crews

This is **a different problem profile** than a CPG plant. The throughput density is higher; the trailer dwell pattern is more cyclical (2 sharp peaks per 24-hour window); and the cost of a delayed outbound trailer hits restaurant service levels directly — every minute lost in the yard means a restaurant misses its breakfast prep window.

## 4. Known Logistics Pain Points

### 1. Florence Facility — A Live Problem, Right Now
The Q3 FY26 call language was unusually specific. PFG is running **legacy and new DCs in parallel** during the Cheney customer migration, and the financial penalty showed up in a publicly-disclosed earnings miss. Anything that:
- Reduces dock dwell at Florence
- Lets the legacy DC handle more volume per door per day
- Compresses the migration window

…directly addresses a problem the CFO acknowledged on a public earnings call. **This is the cleanest "right place, right time" pitch in the dossier.**

### 2. The Outbound Multi-Stop Pattern
Foodservice broadline trucks run loops, not point-to-point. A single Performance Foodservice truck might make 12 stops in 8 hours, mixing:
- Frozen compartment (ice cream, frozen proteins)
- Refrigerated compartment (dairy, produce, fresh meat)
- Dry compartment (canned goods, paper, smallwares)

The **morning yard-out** at a PFG DC is the highest-pressure window of the day. Drivers want to be loaded and on the road by 4–5am to hit restaurant receiving windows. If yard staging is chaotic — bobtailing, hooking, paperwork, gate queue — the cascading impact is **lost restaurant service**, not just internal waste.

### 3. Inbound Manufacturer Trailer Density
PFG receives from thousands of CPG manufacturers. Inbound trailers compete with outbound returns and yard moves for limited dock real estate. At a typical broadline DC, a Tyson reefer might sit 4+ hours waiting for a door because crew is prioritizing outbound staging — that detention bill flows back through the supplier to the cost-of-goods.

### 4. Cheney Migration Wave Mechanics
The "four-wave customer transition" referenced on the Q3 FY26 call is itself a yard-management problem. Each wave shifts customer volume between facilities, which means:
- Trailer pool re-balancing
- New routes, new stop patterns, new yard hold times
- Crews learning new SKU mixes and pick paths
- **More yard moves per dock turn** during transition

This is precisely the operational regime where a yard-visibility tool earns its keep.

### 5. Driver Wait Time = Carrier Tender Behavior
Dylan's old employer (Estes) lives or dies by tender acceptance economics. He knows that a shipper with a reputation for slow yards loses spot capacity in tight markets and pays more in contract negotiation. With ~$3B+ a year flowing through inbound carriers across 155 DCs, **even a 10-minute reduction in average detention is a non-trivial procurement lever** — and one a former pricing analyst will quantify on a napkin.

### 6. Multi-Temperature Compartment Compatibility
Foodservice trucks have multi-temp boxes. Loading sequence matters: the freeze door must load last (drops first), refrigerated middle, dry first. Yard staging that pulls the wrong trailer to the wrong door breaks loading sequence and forces re-handling. A YMS that maps trailer + temp + door capability is non-negotiable infrastructure for this industry — and PFG is running 155 of these without a unified one.

### 7. Vistar's Different Profile
Vistar runs **smaller-package, higher-frequency** distribution to vending operators, theaters, c-stores. Different vehicle types (smaller box trucks, sprinter vans), different loading patterns, but **same yard congestion at the same DCs** because Vistar shares facilities with broadline in some markets [unverified — specific shared facilities].
