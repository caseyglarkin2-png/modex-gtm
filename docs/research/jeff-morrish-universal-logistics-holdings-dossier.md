## 4. The 3PL Adjustment — Why ULH Is a Partnership Target, Not a YMS Sale

### YardFlow's 3PL Thesis (vs. End-Shipper Pitch)
For end-shippers like General Mills or Ford direct, YardFlow is a tool they buy and operate at their own facilities. For a 3PL like Universal Logistics — and specifically for **Logistics Insight Corporation** which runs operations *inside customer plants* — YardFlow is something fundamentally different:

> **A capability LINC buys once and embeds into its plant-side operating model, billable through to the shipper as part of the value-added services contract.**

LINC's day job is operating sequencing centers, sub-assembly plants, parts-marshalling yards, and returnable-container loops for automotive OEMs. Every one of those operations is **yard-and-dock-dense**. Trailers stage, parts containers cycle, OEM milk-runs hit windowed delivery slots, line-side replenishment is choreographed to plant takt time. The yard is the operating system.

LINC already embeds WMS into its operating model. They already operate yard management at the plant. The question is whether the **next layer** of yard execution — real-time, mobile-first, driver-facing, shipper-facing — is a Kaleris/PINC procurement decision or a **YardFlow partnership** that gives LINC a differentiated answer to put in front of OEM customers.

### Why This Is a Partnership Conversation, Not a Vendor Sale
A standard YMS pitch says: *"Replace your spreadsheet with our software."* That's a procurement conversation routed to ULH corporate IT and likely to die in evaluation against Kaleris (PINC), which is the named market leader in this category.

The Jeff Morrish pitch needs to say:

> *"Embed our yard-execution layer into LINC's standard value-added services playbook. We're designed for the multi-site, OEM-plant operating model you already run. You bring the plant-floor operating reputation, the GM-Supplier-of-the-Year credibility, and the next RFP. We bring the execution layer that turns your gate-to-dock story into a competitive differentiator the OEM hasn't seen from Kaleris."*

This reframes Jeff's evaluation:
- Not: *"Should we evaluate this against PINC?"* (procurement instinct: route to IT and Patrick Cox's org)
- Instead: *"Is this a tool I can put in our next pursuit response?"* (Business Development instinct: lean in)

### LINC vs. Corporate ULH — Why The Subsidiary Door Beats The Holding-Co Door
Going to ULH corporate (Tim Phillips, Patrick Cox, Michael Rogers) puts YardFlow in a category fight against PINC/Kaleris where ULH is one buyer evaluating one tool. Going to **LINC Business Development** changes the buying motion entirely:

- LINC wins customer-by-customer plant operations contracts
- Each contract has its own technology spec, often shaped by the customer but increasingly shaped by what LINC brings to the pursuit
- A partnership where LINC standardizes YardFlow into its **value-added services bid template** unlocks N customer deployments, not one
- The economics: LINC bills it through to the OEM as part of the operating model; YardFlow gets multi-plant footprint inside one of the largest plant-side 3PL operators in North America

Other ULH contacts route to procurement (Patrick Cox), finance (Rogers), or top-of-funnel sales (Gargasoulas). Jeff's job — Director, Supply Chain Business Development at LINC — is the **pre-sale solution-design seat**. His currency is what he can put in front of the next OEM RFP. The question is not whether ULH corporate needs YardFlow centrally — they have a functioning YMS stack and a CFO transition. The question is whether **LINC's OEM customers** would value a LINC-operated yard-execution layer as part of their next plant-side contract, and whether YardFlow is the right partner to build it with.

That's the conversation to have with Jeff.
