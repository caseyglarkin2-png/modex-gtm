## 4. Known Logistics Pain Points (The Nestlé USA-Specific View)

### 1. Solon Network Reshuffling = Volume Concentration Elsewhere

The 2023–2024 Solon layoffs explicitly shifted production **to other US sites**. That means Gaffney SC, Jonesboro AR, and the other frozen plants are absorbing more volume into the same dock surface. **More trailers, same docks, manual yard coordination.** This is happening *right now*, on Jeff Kurtenbach's desk.

### 2. Multi-Temperature Dock Contention at Frozen-Heavy Sites

At Solon, Jonesboro, and Gaffney: Stouffer's, Lean Cuisine, Hot Pockets, DiGiorno, Tombstone, Jack's Pizza, Sweet Earth all flow through the same campus. These are all frozen — but they run on **different retail-customer service windows** (club store DCs vs grocery DCs vs foodservice distributors). Trailer prioritization on the yard is a scheduling problem, not a temperature problem. A misroute is a service-level miss, not a quality event — but at retailer chargeback rates, the math still hurts.

### 3. Out-of-Home (Foodservice) Lane Mixing

The Solon plant is now running dedicated lines for the **Out-of-Home / Nestlé Professional** business. Foodservice goes to **different customers** (Sysco, US Foods, Performance Food Group) with **different appointment systems** than retail-DC outbound. When retail and foodservice trailers compete for the same yard surface, you need a yard layer that distinguishes the two — most legacy YMS deployments don't natively.

### 4. Glendale AZ New DC Ramp-Up

The brand-new Glendale beverage factory + DC opened mid-2024. **New facilities run 15–25% below optimal dock utilization in their first 12–18 months.** Standard operating procedures are still being written. **This is the cleanest possible greenfield yard SOP embed opportunity in CPG North America right now** — and it's a single-roof factory-plus-DC, which means the yard sees both inbound raw material trailers and outbound finished-goods trailers on the same surface. Yard orchestration matters here more than at single-purpose plants.

### 5. Coffee Creamer Velocity vs Frozen Meal Cycle Time

Coffee mate ships in different patterns than a frozen DiGiorno: higher SKU velocity, faster grocery DC turnover, shorter dwell tolerance. If Anderson IN and Glendale AZ are running shared lanes with the frozen network through any of the 3PL DCs, **the velocity mismatch creates dock contention.** Faster-moving SKUs queue behind slower-moving frozen pallets. A yard view that distinguishes by SKU velocity matters.

### 6. PINC/Kaleris Legacy Footprint — Confirmed Customer Signal

Nestlé appears in the **Kaleris/PINC Daimler case study customer list** (named in the 2025 PDF). This is a probable-customer signal at the Nestlé Group level, not a confirmed deployment-site-specific Nestlé USA reference — but it's enough that **Nestlé operators are likely familiar with the PINC/Kaleris yard category.** The pitch shape is not "introduce yard management" — it's "the next-generation operating model after PINC."

Specifically: **PINC/Kaleris solved site-level yard visibility a decade ago.** What it doesn't solve is the network-level standardization problem — every site running its own gate workflow, every plant having its own spotter pattern, the Solon supply chain team trying to get a unified network view across 20+ US factories. **That's where the displacement story lives.**

### 7. The Single-Manufacturing-System Network Effect

Nestlé Group has scaled its **single manufacturing system to ~90% of 335 global factories**. That's a unified MES/automation layer inside the plant. But the **yard sits outside the plant** — between the gate and the dock — and is not part of that system. Every plant having a different yard workflow undercuts the value of the unified inside-plant system. **YardFlow's `flowNETWORK` is the yard-side analog of what Nestlé has already built for the inside-plant side.**

### 8. The 3PL DC Coordination Layer

Nestlé USA doesn't operate its own outbound DC network at scale — it works with 3PLs (Penske historically; others unconfirmed). **Yard operations at 3PL-operated DCs are typically the most fragmented part of any CPG supply chain.** Each 3PL site runs its own gate, dock, and spotter routine. Standardizing across owned plants AND 3PL DCs is harder than standardizing across owned only. This is where the displacement story actually pays off.
