# The yard is the last part of your supply chain you stopped measuring.

We think you did it on purpose.

You measure the truck. You measure the dock. You measure the pallet, the SKU, the sell-through, the on-time-in-full. You have a TMS, a WMS, an OMS, and three flavors of business intelligence reading from each of them.

But the yard? The five-acre rectangle of asphalt where every shipment in your network spends fourteen percent of its life? That you measure with a clipboard and a guy named Tony.

It's not an oversight. It's a strategy. The yard is where decades of capex have been written off as "the cost of doing business," and the longer that math stays unmeasured, the longer it stays uncontested.

## What we know.

Kraft Heinz has thirty-six manufacturing and distribution sites. Each one runs about eight detention events a day. Each event is roughly ninety minutes of dwell time at a hundred-and-twenty-dollar load rate. That math is a quarter of a million dollars a month per facility — nine million a year across the network — and none of it is on a P&L line called "yard."

It's filed under labor variance, fuel cost, OS&D, customer chargeback, and "Q3 was just a tough quarter." It is the largest unaccounted-for cost center in your supply chain, and it has been for as long as you've owned the brand.

PINC and Kaleris proved the yard *matters*. They sold you on visibility — a digital map of which trailer is in which spot. That was the first problem. It's solved.

## The next problem.

The next problem is the network. It's that every site runs differently. Every gate has its own protocol. Every dock office has its own paper. Every yard has its own clipboard, its own Tony, its own variance — and no two of them can be compared.

A site-level YMS gives you thirty-six dashboards. A Yard Network System gives you one. One protocol, one gate experience, one driver journey, one set of receipts. Every site doing the same thing the same way, measured against the same line.

> The yard is not a real estate problem. The yard is a protocol problem.

When the protocol is the same, you can compare. When you can compare, you can compete sites against each other. When you can compete sites, the worst one improves toward the best one. That's the network effect on yard cost — and you don't get it from a YMS, you get it from a *network*.

## What we'd do.

We'd start with one of your sites. Not the worst one — the one with a champion. We'd cut over the gate, the driver journey, and the dBOL in thirty days. We'd run the new protocol parallel to the old for a week, then turn the old one off.

By month three, we'd have a baseline that holds across every site you point us at. By month twelve, you'd have a single number — yards-per-truck-hour, or whatever you want to call it — that gets reviewed in your quarterly operating cadence next to OTIF and inventory turns. The yard would graduate from "the cost of doing business" to a board-level operating lever.

That's it. That's the manifesto. The yard is the last part of your supply chain you stopped measuring. We think you did it on purpose.

We think it's time to measure it.
