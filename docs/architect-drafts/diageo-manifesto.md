# The yard is the last part of your supply chain you stopped measuring.

We think you did it on purpose.

Diageo measures the bottle, the case, the pallet, the shipment, the chargeback. You have a finance function that can tell you the GP delta on a single SKU at a single retailer in a single quarter.

But the yard? The asphalt where every spirits shipment in your network sits between production and distribution? You measure that with paperwork and a clipboard.

It's not an oversight. It's a strategy. Yard cost has been bundled into "logistics overhead" for so long that the business has stopped asking what it actually is.

## What we know.

Diageo runs roughly thirty-one production and distribution sites across the network. Each one runs about eight detention events a day. At ninety-minute average dwell and a hundred-and-twenty-dollar load rate, that's about a quarter of a million dollars a month, per facility — seven and a half million a year, network-wide — and none of it is on a P&L line called "yard."

The category leader (PINC/Kaleris) sold you on visibility. That solved the first problem.

## The next problem.

The next problem is that every site runs differently. The bottling plant in Plainfield runs its yard one way. The DC in Tracy runs it another. The bonded warehouse in Norwalk runs it a third. They have different gates, different paper, different protocols, different exception handling — and as a result, you cannot compare them.

You cannot compare them. So you cannot compete them. So you cannot improve them.

## What we'd do.

Standardize the gate, dock office, driver journey, and bill of lading across the network. One protocol, one system, one set of receipts. The bottling plants and the DCs and the bonded warehouses all running the same yard, measured against the same line.

The result, at the comparable scale we've shipped: four to seven percent throughput improvement on the same headcount. That math, applied to thirty-one sites, is a renewable seven-figure operating dividend.

That's the manifesto.

The yard is the last part of your supply chain you stopped measuring. We think you did it on purpose. We think it's time to measure it.
