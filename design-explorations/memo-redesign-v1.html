§ 01·The thesis

The yard is the network's most expensive blind spot.

Across multi-site supply chain networks, the gate-to-dock layer is the last manually coordinated handoff between trucks, dock doors, and shift schedules. In most networks it still runs on radios, paper, and local convention. The cost shows up in detention spend, dock-office headcount, and shelf life that quietly burns before the case is even picked.[1]

We call this layer the Yard Network System (YNS). It is to the dock what a TMS is to procurement: a system of record for execution. Most enterprises do not have one. The ones that do tend to recover the investment in under six months because the savings come from labor, carrier accessorials, and throughput at the same time.[2]

The premise of the brief that follows is straightforward. Dannon's North American network has the structural conditions where the yard layer is the binding constraint on freshness and throughput today. A network-grade YNS closes that gap with a single operating protocol across every facility — not site-by-site reinvention.

Aside

"Network-grade" matters here. Single-site YMS deployments routinely show measured improvement, but the savings stay local. Network-grade visibility is what unlocks planning, procurement, and S&OP leverage.

Carrier model
Contract carriers and 3PL — no private fleet

The constraint we keep coming back to is shelf life. Fresh dairy and plant-based both run on a 14-day clock, and the clock starts well before the case is picked. When a reefer sits an extra 30–45 minutes in the yard during summer, that time comes out of product life you never get back — and the cost shows up in shrink, service recovery, and carrier scorecards at the same time.[3]

Underneath that, each plant runs its own gate, dock, and spotter coordination, so the White Plains supply chain team has to stitch a network view from 13 different local routines. That fragmentation is the part that compounds: the more facilities Dannon runs to a different yard standard, the harder it gets to act on the freshness data once you have it.

Caveat

This is what public data and our research surface. We may be wrong about parts of it — the most useful thing you can do with this is push back on the parts that don't match what you see internally on detention spend, dock-zone discipline, or how much the yard layer is already standardized today.

Sources used in this analysis, with confidence levels shipped inline so you can see exactly which claims are anchored in measured data and which are extrapolated from public sources.

  3. Primo Brands operating data [under NDA] Measured

    Post-deployment turn time, dock-office headcount, and per-site profit impact have been shared with us by the Primo CFO and ops team. Specific numbers are referenceable in a peer call.

What we don't know

  • Your real detention spend by lane and carrier — we estimate, you measure
  • Spotter and dock-office FTE structure per site, including overtime mix
  • Internal SLAs between plants, DCs, and the Away From Home / retail customers
  • Where temperature-zone misassignment events are concentrated today
  • How much of this layer is already covered by an existing YMS at any of the 13 sites
